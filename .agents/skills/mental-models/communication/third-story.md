# Third Story

## What it is

The Third Story is the account a neutral, impartial observer would give of a conflict or situation. It requires stepping back from both your perspective and the other party's to see the event as an outside audience would interpret it.

## Core principle

By learning to describe the gap between your story and the other person's story you dramatically increase empathy and understanding. Acknowledging this gap aloud signals objectivity, which disarms defensiveness and opens collaboration.

## When to invoke

- Involved in a difficult personal disagreement or tense business negotiation
- Conducting a mid-mortem or post-mortem and need to analyse your own work objectively
- Two parties hold irreconcilable narratives about the same event
- Need to de-escalate a conflict before it entrenches further

## How to apply

1. **Visualise a recording** -- imagine a complete recording of the situation exists
2. **Adopt the outsider's view** -- ask what an impartial audience would say was happening if they watched or listened
3. **Describe the gap** -- clearly articulate the difference between your perspective and the other person's
4. **Acknowledge the gap aloud** -- present the third story to the other party to signal willingness to consider an objective view, encouraging reciprocal openness

## Anti-patterns

- **Assuming you must agree** -- acknowledging the other person's story does not mean surrendering your own; the Third Story describes the difference, not a concession
- **Fundamental attribution error** -- falling back on the assumption that the other person's actions stem from character flaws while excusing your own via self-serving bias; this traps you in your own story
