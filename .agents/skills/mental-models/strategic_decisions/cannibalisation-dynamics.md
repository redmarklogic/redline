# Cannibalisation Dynamics

## What it is

Cannibalisation Dynamics describes the strategic trap where a company's decision to sell or license a tool that automates its own core service undermines its competitive advantage more than the new revenue stream compensates. The company effectively hands competitors the means to erode the moat it spent years building.

## Core principle

The value of keeping a capability internal often exceeds the revenue from selling it. When a capability is the source of your competitive differentiation, distributing it levels the playing field — turning a strategic asset into a commodity. The calculation is asymmetric: licensing revenue is bounded and one-time; the competitive loss is compounding and permanent.

## When to invoke

- Evaluating whether a company will commercialise an internal tool or automation
- Assessing a build-vs-buy dynamic where an incumbent could in principle become a supplier
- Deciding whether to open-source, license, or productise proprietary internal capabilities
- Analysing why a strong incumbent has not entered an adjacent market despite having the capability
- Identifying market gaps created by incumbents who rationally choose not to cannibalise

## How to apply

1. **Identify the moat.** What competitive advantage does the internal capability provide? Is it speed, quality, risk reduction, or cost?
2. **Price the asymmetry.** Estimate the licensing revenue ceiling vs the strategic loss floor. If competitors gaining the capability would compress margins or erode client retention, the loss floor is likely larger.
3. **Check the incentive structure.** Ask: does the organisation's revenue model depend on the output the tool automates? If yes, default to "will not sell" unless the firm is explicitly exiting that service line.
4. **Look for the rational gap.** When incumbents will not cannibalise, the market gap is real and defensible by a third party — this is where new entrants can win.
5. **PSF application:** For professional services firms (engineering, legal, accounting), whose revenue is billable hours, cannibalisation is structural. A Tier 1 engineering firm selling a report-automation tool to Tier 3 competitors would directly reduce its own competitive advantage. Default answer: they will not sell.

## Anti-patterns

- **Assuming capability implies willingness to sell** — large firms often have the best internal tools and the least incentive to commercialise them
- **Treating licensing as pure upside** — every capability sold to a competitor is a reduction in relative advantage; it is not free revenue
- **Applying SaaS logic to PSFs** — software companies can scale distribution without cannibalising their own revenue; professional services firms cannot

## Related models

- **Trade-off Analysis** — the formal framework for pricing the asymmetry between licensing revenue and competitive loss
- **Reversible vs Irreversible Decisions** — licensing a capability is often irreversible; once competitors have it, the moat is gone
