# Value-Effort Matrix

## What it is

A 2x2 prioritisation matrix that plots potential features or initiatives on two axes -- **Value** (the benefit to the customer and organisation) and **Effort** (the total cost to deliver across all functions, not just engineering). The objective is to identify and act on the high-value, low-effort quadrant first.

## Core principle

Return on investment applied to the product backlog: do the most leveraged things first. Value is defined holistically as Customer Need + Organisation's Goals. Effort is the full-organisation cost -- include marketing, sales, and partnerships, not just engineering hours. When the backlog grows large, translate the visual matrix into a numerical ROI Scorecard by adding a Confidence multiplier.

## When to invoke

- Initial triage of a backlog where gut-feel prioritisation is being used
- Quick visual alignment session where a team needs to agree on relative priority
- When a backlog has fewer than approximately 30 items (beyond that, the matrix becomes cluttered; use the ROI Scorecard formula instead)
- When stakeholders disagree on relative importance and a shared visual is needed

## How to apply

1. Define **Value** for each idea: score Customer Need and Business Objective separately, then sum them
2. Define **Effort** for each idea: use T-shirt sizing (XS=1, S=2, M=3, L=4, XL=5) to estimate total organisational cost
3. Plot each idea on the 2x2 grid: Value on the Y-axis (higher = more value), Effort on the X-axis (lower = less effort)
4. Prioritise items in the high-value, low-effort quadrant first
5. When the matrix becomes cluttered (more than 20-30 items), convert to the ROI Scorecard formula: **(Customer Need + Business Objective) / Effort x Confidence = Priority Score**

## Anti-patterns

- **Engineering-only effort estimates** -- understates true organisational cost and inflates apparent ROI
- **Using the matrix alone for large backlogs** -- a 2x2 grid cannot differentiate 50+ items reliably; escalate to the numerical ROI Scorecard
- **Treating Value as revenue alone** -- value must include customer need satisfaction, not just monetisation potential
- **Skipping Confidence** -- proceeding without discounting for unknowns inflates confidence in the priority order
