# Kano Model

## What it is

A customer-need classification framework developed by Dr. Noriaki Kano that categorises features into three tiers based on how customers perceive their value: **Expected (Dissatisfiers)**, **Normal (Satisfiers)**, and **Exciting (Delighters)**. Satisfaction is non-linear: absent dissatisfiers destroy perceived quality, present satisfiers scale proportionally, and delighters create disproportionate joy when present but cause no dissatisfaction when absent.

## Core principle

Customer satisfaction depends on which tier of need is addressed. Covering only Expected needs is insufficient in competitive markets -- customers still rate the product as mediocre. Normal needs drive satisfaction linearly. Exciting needs create category leadership. Critically, expectations rise over time: today's Delighter becomes tomorrow's Expected need as the market evolves.

## When to invoke

- Deciding whether to invest in table-stakes improvements vs differentiation features
- Assessing why a feature is not generating the expected customer satisfaction lift
- Evaluating competitive positioning: what constitutes the current Expected baseline vs competitors' Exciting tier?
- When customer research reveals diminishing returns from continued investment in an area

## How to apply

1. Classify each candidate feature into one of three tiers:
   - **Expected (Dissatisfiers)**: Must be present for the customer to consider the product viable; absence causes rejection, but presence does not generate positive sentiment. Maps to MoSCoW Must-have.
   - **Normal (Satisfiers)**: Presence satisfies and absence disappoints proportionally; the customer explicitly asks for these. Maps broadly to Should-have.
   - **Exciting (Delighters)**: The customer did not ask for them and will not miss them if absent, but their presence creates disproportionate delight. Maps to Could-have.
2. Check customer context -- a Delighter for one segment may be irrelevant for another (e.g., rain-sensing wipers for customers in an arid climate)
3. Assess temporal drift: which current Expected needs were Delighters 3-5 years ago? Which current Delighters are likely to become Expected within 2-3 years?
4. Cover all Expected needs first; then invest in Normal needs; then differentiate with Exciting needs

## Anti-patterns

- **Confusing Exciting with high-value** -- Expected needs have higher must-have value than Exciting needs; prioritising Delighters before dissatisfiers kills retention
- **Treating the model as static** -- Delighters commoditise; refresh classification regularly as market expectations evolve
- **Applying without customer knowledge** -- Kano classification is perception-based; without deep customer understanding, tier assignments are guesses, not facts
- **Using Kano as a standalone prioritisation tool** -- it classifies the type of value, not its magnitude; combine with Value-Effort or ROI Scorecard for ranking
