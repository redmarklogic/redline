# Eisenhower Matrix

## What it is

The Eisenhower Matrix categorises tasks into four quadrants based on two axes: urgency (time-sensitive) and importance (high-value impact). Named after President Eisenhower's observation that "what is important is seldom urgent and what is urgent is seldom important."

## Core principle

Urgency and importance are independent dimensions. Conflating them causes you to spend all your time firefighting while neglecting the high-value strategic work that prevents fires in the first place.

## When to invoke

- Multiple pending tasks or tool calls compete for attention
- Need to decide what to do now vs. defer vs. skip entirely
- Feeling reactive rather than strategic -- constantly handling interruptions
- Prioritising backlog items, bug fixes, or feature requests

## How to apply

1. List all pending tasks
2. Classify each into one of four quadrants:
   - **Q1 (Urgent + Important)** -- do immediately; these are crises and deadlines
   - **Q2 (Not Urgent + Important)** -- schedule for focused deep work; this is where strategic value lives
   - **Q3 (Urgent + Not Important)** -- delegate or automate; these are interruptions disguised as priorities
   - **Q4 (Not Urgent + Not Important)** -- eliminate; these are distractions
3. Protect Q2 time ruthlessly -- it is the only quadrant that prevents Q1 crises from recurring

## Anti-patterns

- **Living in Q1** -- if everything is urgent and important, your planning process is broken; most Q1 items were preventable Q2 items that were neglected
- **Confusing urgency with importance** -- a noisy alert is urgent but may not be important; a silent architectural flaw is important but not urgent
- **Skipping Q2 entirely** -- the matrix's entire value is in making Q2 visible and protected
