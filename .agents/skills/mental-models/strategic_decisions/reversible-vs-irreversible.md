# Reversible vs Irreversible Decisions

## What it is

This framework categorises choices based on whether they can be easily undone ("two-way doors") or are permanent and highly consequential ("one-way doors"). It determines how much deliberation a decision deserves.

## Core principle

Decisions are not created equal. Irreversible choices must be made methodically and slowly; reversible choices should be made quickly to avoid stalling progress.

## When to invoke

- Experiencing tension between deciding quickly and wanting more information
- Analysis paralysis is blocking progress
- Organisational bureaucracy is stifling experimentation and speed
- Need to calibrate how much process to apply to a given choice

## How to apply

1. **Assess permanence** -- determine whether the decision is a one-way door (hard or impossible to unwind) or a two-way door (changeable)
2. **Fast-track reversible choices** -- decide quickly and fluidly; if it turns out suboptimal, step back through the door and change course
3. **Slow down for irreversible choices** -- deliberate methodically, consult broadly, and gather evidence before committing

## Anti-patterns

- **One-way process for two-way doors** -- applying a heavyweight, cautious process to every decision; leads to risk aversion, slowness, and diminished invention
- **Analysis paralysis** -- over-analysing reversible decisions in search of perfection; by not choosing, you are passively choosing the status quo, which may be worse
