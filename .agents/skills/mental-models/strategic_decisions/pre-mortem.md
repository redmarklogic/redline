# Pre-mortem

## What it is

A pre-mortem is a prospective technique in which, before committing to a plan, the team assumes the project has already failed spectacularly and then works backward to identify every plausible cause. It is the proactive counterpart to the postmortem.

## Core principle

Prospective hindsight: by artificially treating failure as a fact rather than a possibility, you bypass optimistic probability bias and create psychological safety for team members to voice hidden doubts before it is too late to act on them.

## When to invoke

- Immediately before a final commitment is made or a launch is executed
- When a team has reached rapid consensus and appears overconfident
- When there is a risk of groupthink suppressing dissenting views
- After completing a risk register but wanting to surface unknown unknowns

## How to apply

1. Frame the scenario: tell the group "it is six months from now and the project has failed completely -- what happened?"
2. Generate reasons independently and silently: each participant writes their list before discussion, preventing anchoring to the loudest voice in the room
3. Consolidate: go around the group, collect one item per person per round until all unique failure modes are captured
4. Prioritise: identify the highest-probability or highest-impact risks from the consolidated list
5. Adjust the plan: make concrete changes to mitigate the top risks before committing

## Anti-patterns

- **Standard risk-list framing** -- asking "what might go wrong?" instead of "it did go wrong, why?" produces conservative, expected answers and misses creative failure modes
- **No psychological safety** -- if team members fear criticism for raising concerns, the exercise becomes superficial and the real risks stay hidden
- **Box-checking** -- conducting the exercise but failing to act on the identified risks before launch; this is worse than not running the exercise at all
