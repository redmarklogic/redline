# Black Swan

## What it is

A Black Swan is a highly improbable, unpredictable event that shatters prior assumptions and has a massive, disproportionate impact. The term comes from the centuries-old Western belief that all swans were white -- overturned when black swans were discovered in Australia.

## Core principle

The past is not a reliable predictor of the future in complex, fat-tailed domains. Rare extreme events fall outside normal expectations, so standard probability tools will underestimate their risk by orders of magnitude.

## When to invoke

- Operating in volatile, complex environments (financial markets, geopolitics, infrastructure)
- Evaluating systemic or catastrophic risk
- Relying heavily on historical data to guarantee future safety
- Designing systems that must survive unknown unknowns

## How to apply

1. **Recognise the domain** -- determine whether you are in a bell-curve domain (e.g. human height) or a fat-tailed domain (e.g. wealth, natural disasters) where outliers dominate outcomes
2. **Stop predicting, start preparing** -- accept that forecasting rare high-impact events is impossible; prepare for the fact that they will happen
3. **Question assumptions** -- continuously challenge what you take for granted; remember Russell's chicken who assumed humans were kind because it was fed every day
4. **Cultivate antifragility** -- avoid risks that could wipe you out entirely while exposing yourself to upside optionality (worst case: nothing happens; best case: outsized gain)

## Anti-patterns

- **Bell-curve trap** -- applying normal distributions to fat-tailed domains, underestimating catastrophe risk by factors of 10 to 1,000
- **Hindsight bias** -- believing after a Black Swan event that it was obvious and predictable all along, which masks how fragile your forecasting actually is
- **Inductive fallacy** -- assuming that a lack of past extreme events guarantees future stability

## Source

*The Black Swan: The Impact of the Highly Improbable* and *Antifragile* -- Nassim Nicholas Taleb
