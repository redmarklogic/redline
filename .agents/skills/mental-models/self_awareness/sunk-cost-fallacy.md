# Sunk-Cost Fallacy

## What it is

The sunk-cost fallacy is the cognitive trap of allowing irrecoverable past investments -- money, time, or effort -- to influence current decisions. Because those resources are already gone regardless of any future action, they are objectively irrelevant to rational forward-looking choice.

## Core principle

Past costs cannot be retrieved. Rational decisions are made solely on the future potential and opportunity cost of each option, not on what has already been spent.

## When to invoke

- Continuing a failing project, strategy, or relationship because of prior effort invested
- Hesitating to abandon work because giving up feels like admitting a loss
- Evaluating whether to refactor, pivot, or cancel something that is underperforming
- Noticing the thought "we've come too far to stop now"

## How to apply

1. Acknowledge that past costs are gone -- they cannot be recovered regardless of future action
2. Identify loss aversion at work: the brain feels losses more acutely than equivalent gains, biasing you toward continuation
3. Re-evaluate objectively: take a realistic look at the actual probability of future success
4. Apply opportunity cost: would those same resources generate more value applied elsewhere?
5. Decide to continue or stop based purely on future expected returns -- not on what was already spent

## Anti-patterns

- **Escalation of commitment (Concorde fallacy)** -- pouring further resources into a doomed effort because of prior investment; the supersonic Concorde programme is the canonical example
- **The pride trap** -- holding on because stopping would feel like a public admission of failure; this often causes more reputational damage in the long run than an early, clean exit
- **Anchoring to purchase price** -- refusing to sell an asset below what you paid for it, letting an arbitrary historical number override present-value reasoning
