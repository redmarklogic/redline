# Dunning-Kruger Effect

## What it is

The Dunning-Kruger effect is a cognitive bias in which people with low ability in a domain overestimate their competence, while people with high ability tend to underestimate theirs. Named after social psychologists David Dunning and Justin Kruger, it describes the confidence curve as someone moves from novice to expert: a sharp early spike of overconfidence, a valley of humility as real complexity becomes visible, and a gradual recovery as genuine expertise is built.

## Core principle

Incompetence masks itself. You cannot accurately assess your own skill level in a domain you do not yet understand. The test of whether you are in the danger zone is the feeling of unwarranted certainty — if a decision feels obvious in unfamiliar territory, that confidence is the warning signal, not the green light.

## When to invoke

- Feeling confident about a decision in a domain where you have little experience
- Assessing a team member's skill level before assigning them independent work
- Evaluating your own reasoning when operating at the boundary of your Circle of Competence
- Noticing that a junior agent or colleague is not asking questions they should be asking
- Reviewing AI-generated output in a domain requiring expert judgment

## How to apply

1. **Gate on Circle of Competence first.** Are you a Lifer (deep experiential knowledge) or a Stranger in this domain? Confidence in a Stranger is the Dunning-Kruger signal.
2. **Name the novice spike.** When you feel certain about a complex problem early, explicitly ask: "Am I in the first spike of this curve?"
3. **Invite calibration.** Seek out someone with genuine expertise and expose your reasoning. The gap between their response and your expectation is the measure of your blind spot.
4. **Track the valley.** As real competence grows, confidence dips before recovering. The dip is a sign of progress, not failure — it means you can now see what you could not see before.
5. **Coach the curve.** When working with people at the early spike, praise learning progression without reinforcing overconfidence. Help them see the complexity without discouraging effort.

## Anti-patterns

- **Mistaking fluency for expertise** — being able to talk about a topic confidently after brief exposure is not the same as understanding it deeply; this is the core novice trap
- **Dismissing the effect as applying only to others** — experienced people can enter the novice spike in adjacent domains they incorrectly assume they understand
- **Conflating humility with incompetence** — genuine experts often sound uncertain; low confidence is not the same as low ability

## Related models

- **Circle of Competence** — the upstream gate: define your territory before acting
- **Cognitive Biases** — broader set of systematic reasoning errors; Dunning-Kruger is a specific instance
