# Cognitive Biases

## What it is

Cognitive biases are systematic, unconscious errors of judgement hardwired by evolution that distort our objective view of reality and silently influence our decisions. Key biases include confirmation bias, anchoring, availability bias, sunk-cost fallacy, and the framing effect.

## Core principle

Human brains default to fast, intuitive "System 1" thinking driven by vivid, readily available experiences, which frequently overrides deliberate "System 2" reasoning. To make objective decisions you must recognise these blind spots, distrust initial impressions, and deliberately slow down.

## When to invoke

- Making significant choices with limited or ambiguous information
- Evaluating new data that challenges existing beliefs
- Relying heavily on gut feeling in an unfamiliar domain
- Deciding whether to continue or abandon a struggling project
- Negotiating, where anchors and framing are actively used against you

## How to apply

1. **Assume you are wrong** -- do not trust first impressions, anecdotes, or immediate gut reactions
2. **Slow down** -- shift from impulsive System 1 to deliberate System 2 thinking; re-read information and evaluate rationally before acting
3. **Think grey** -- delay judgement; avoid categorising instantly as good/bad until you have gathered objective facts
4. **Seek the devil's advocate** -- actively look for the opposite perspective and involve people with differing views
5. **Evaluate on opportunity cost** -- to defeat sunk-cost fallacy, assess options based only on future return, ignoring what has already been spent

## Anti-patterns

- **Blindly trusting your gut** -- relying on intuition in unfamiliar or complex situations where you lack real expertise
- **Backfire effect** -- doubling down on a disproven belief when confronted with contradicting evidence
- **Cognitive dissonance** -- rationalising errors to protect ego instead of admitting mistakes
- **Filter bubble** -- surrounding yourself only with confirming voices and media, which amplifies availability and confirmation biases
