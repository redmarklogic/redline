# Second-Order Thinking

## What it is

Second-order thinking is the practice of looking beyond the immediate consequences of an action to consider the subsequent ripple effects -- the "effects of the effects" -- across time and interconnected systems.

## Core principle

You can never merely do one thing. Every action triggers a chain reaction in a connected world, so evaluating downstream consequences is essential to sound decision-making.

## When to invoke

- Making decisions in tightly connected systems where short-term gains may hide long-term pain
- Needing to delay immediate gratification to serve long-term interests
- Building cooperative trust for future outcomes
- Constructing arguments that must anticipate future challenges

## How to apply

1. Identify the first-order consequence of the proposed action
2. Ask "and then what?" -- project second-, third-, and higher-order consequences over time
3. Evaluate whether the downstream effects align with your ultimate goals
4. Choose the path that maximises long-term benefit, even if the short-term payoff is less attractive

## Anti-patterns

- **The Cobra Effect** -- stopping at the immediate result and ignoring perverse incentives (e.g. rewarding dead cobras caused people to breed cobras for the bounty)
- **Slippery-slope paralysis** -- assuming any action inevitably cascades to disaster, leading to analysis paralysis instead of evaluating the most likely consequences
