# Probabilistic Thinking

## What it is

Probabilistic thinking is the process of estimating the likelihood of specific outcomes using math, logic, and historical base rates to navigate uncertainty. It replaces false certainty with calibrated expectations about an inherently unpredictable world.

## Core principle

The future is fundamentally unpredictable, but by generating realistic probabilities and preparing for extreme volatility you can position yourself to survive and thrive. The goal is not prediction but preparation.

## When to invoke

- Making decisions under uncertainty or limited information
- Navigating complex social or economic systems
- Evaluating risks (investments, insurance, project planning)
- Operating in volatile environments where rare events can have massive impact

## How to apply

1. **Establish base rates** -- look at historical frequencies for situations like the one you face before analysing the specific event
2. **Use Bayesian updating** -- as new information arrives, adjust your probability estimates rather than treating prior beliefs as fixed
3. **Account for fat tails** -- determine whether you are in a "bell curve" domain (e.g. human height) or a "fat tail" domain (e.g. wealth, natural disasters); in fat-tail domains, prepare for rare extreme events
4. **Evaluate asymmetries** -- assess the "metaprobability" that your estimates themselves are accurate, and check whether you systematically skew optimistic or pessimistic

## Anti-patterns

- **Gambler's fallacy** -- believing independent events are "due" for reversal after a streak
- **Ignoring base rates** -- overreacting to vivid anecdotes without factoring in historical frequency
- **Bell-curve trap** -- assuming normal distributions in fat-tailed domains, underestimating black-swan events by orders of magnitude
- **Overconfidence** -- failing to recognise systematic biases in your own probability estimates
