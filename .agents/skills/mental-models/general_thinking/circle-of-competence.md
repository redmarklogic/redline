# Circle of Competence

## What it is

The Circle of Competence defines the boundary between areas where you have deep, experiential knowledge and areas where you have only surface-level understanding. It separates the domains where you can make accurate decisions from those where you cannot.

## Core principle

Within your circle of competence you know exactly what you do not know. Operating inside this boundary gives you a genuine edge; straying outside it without awareness creates dangerous blind spots.

## When to invoke

- Facing a significant decision and need to assess whether to act independently or defer to an expert
- Forced to operate in an unfamiliar domain
- Evaluating the reliability of advice from an outside professional
- Deciding whether to commit resources to a new area vs. doubling down on existing strengths

## How to apply

1. Acknowledge you are a "Stranger" in the new domain and learn the basics before acting
2. Consult experts who have strong circles of competence; ask detailed questions to probe the limits of their knowledge, not just for answers
3. Use general mental models to augment limited domain-specific knowledge
4. Maintain and expand your circle honestly: keep a performance journal to track failures objectively, and seek external feedback to reveal hidden vulnerabilities

## Anti-patterns

- **Straying without awareness** -- venturing into areas where you "don't even know what you don't know," triggering the Dunning-Kruger effect
- **Treating the circle as static** -- failing to update your competence boundaries as the world changes
- **Faking it** -- mistaking surface exposure (courses, articles) for genuine expertise built through years of experience
- **Blindly trusting experts** -- relying on someone else's circle without considering how their incentives (commissions, quotas) might bias their advice
