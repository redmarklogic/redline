# Five Whys

## What it is

The Five Whys is a root-cause analysis technique that traces a surface-level incident back to its fundamental origin by repeatedly asking "why did that happen?". The number five is a guideline; you keep asking until you reach a structural root cause or falsifiable first principle.

## Core principle

Distinguish between the proximate cause (the immediate trigger) and the root cause (the real underlying reason). Treating the root cause fixes the systemic disease; treating the proximate cause only patches the symptom.

## When to invoke

- Conducting a postmortem on a failed project, incident, or off-track situation
- Need to cut through dogma, cultural myths, or shared assumptions to reach first principles
- A fix has been applied but the same type of failure keeps recurring
- Investigating why a process broke down rather than just who was involved

## How to apply

1. Identify the proximate cause -- the immediate event that triggered the failure
2. Ask "why did that happen?" to find the direct technical or procedural reason
3. Keep asking "why?" to bridge from hardware/technical factors into human and systemic factors
4. Stop when you reach a foundational structural flaw or a falsifiable fact
5. Implement structural changes that address the root cause so the chain reaction cannot recur

## Anti-patterns

- **Stopping at the proximate cause** -- applying a short-term fix and leaving the systemic vulnerability untreated
- **Using it for emotional introspection** -- the tool is designed to land on a concrete "what" or "how", not feelings
- **Settling for assumptions** -- ending at "because I said so" or "it just is" indicates you hit dogma, not a root cause
- **Self-defence** -- becoming defensive when the process exposes your own gaps defeats the entire purpose
