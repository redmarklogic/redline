# Memo: Positioning Language — Reviewer Sensitivity

**Sub-domain**: report-writing
**Last verified**: 2026-04-30
**Confidence**: practitioner-grounded
**Sources**: 25+ years in a large civil engineering consultancy

## To: John (Head of Marketing)

**From**: Graeme
**Date**: 30 April 2026
**Subject**: The "catches what a senior reviewer would catch" line will lose us deals

John,

**The problem.** The line "catches what a senior reviewer would catch" tells a Technical Director (TD) that their reviewers are missing things. That is how they will read it — not as a compliment to the tool, but as an accusation about their team. TDs built those review processes. They trained those people. You are telling them their house is not in order.

**Why this kills the deal.** The TD is the gatekeeper. They do not sign the purchase order, but they can veto it. A TD who feels their competence — or their team's competence — is being questioned will find twelve reasons why the tool is unnecessary. You will never overcome that objection because it is emotional, not rational.

**The dynamic.** Engineers do not buy tools that imply they are failing. They buy tools that free them to do the work they are trained for and enjoy. A senior reviewer stuck checking clause references and formatting is wasted. They know it. Frame the tool as removing drudgery, not replacing judgment.

**My suggested alternative:**

> "Handles the routine compliance checks so your senior reviewers can focus on the engineering judgment only they can provide."

This works because it:

1. Validates the reviewer's expertise ("only they can provide")
2. Names the real pain (routine checks are tedious and beneath their skill level)
3. Positions the tool as subordinate to the engineer, not competing with them

Use this framing or something close to it. I am happy to review the final copy before it goes out.

— Graeme
