# Geotechnical Report Quality Dimensions

**Sub-domain**: report-writing
**Last verified**: 2026-05-13
**Confidence**: cross-referenced
**Sources**: Geotechnical Report Workflows (GRW), Risk Assessment in Engineering (RA),
Geotechnical Baseline Reports (GBR notebook), Ground Engineering Magazine (GEM)

## Summary

Senior geotechnical engineers assess report quality across four layers: mechanical/formatting,
structural completeness, linguistic/liability risk, and technical defensibility. Formal QA
checklists exist (four named checklists in enterprise practice) with the Section B Review
Checklist as the ultimate issuance gate. Six defect categories routinely create professional
liability exposure. GBRs require a distinct, more demanding quality ruleset because they are
binding contract documents. No formal quality scoring or maturity model for geotechnical
reports exists in the industry — quality is managed entirely through checklist-driven peer
review. Portfolio-level quality monitoring is an established industry practice (AECOM, Arup,
Network Rail, Keltbray have all deployed such systems). The five highest-priority portfolio
dimensions for NZ/AU firms are: (1) linguistic/PLI risk compliance, (2) structural
completeness, (3) review sign-off compliance, (4) template and operational discipline, and
(5) standards application signalling. AI-assisted false-positive triage is necessary and
trust-calibrated by finding severity.

## Key Facts

### Four Quality Layers

1. **Layer 1 — Mechanical/Formatting**: font, naming conventions, spelling, figure-text
   linkage. Assessed by BIS administrative staff and Author (self-review).
   Binary checks [GRW: citations 5, 7, 14-18].

2. **Layer 2 — Structural Completeness**: logical sequence, mandatory sections present,
   calculations checked by multiple methods, data tables accurate, drawing stamps correct,
   conclusions following logically from body. Assessed by Author and TR [GRW: citations 5-7,
   19-21].

3. **Layer 3 — Linguistic/Liability Risk**: absence of taboo words that void PLI, presence
   of mandatory disclaimer clauses, scope limitations appropriately worded. Assessed by TR
   and PD. Most consequential for professional indemnity exposure [RA: citations 3-5, 21-25;
   GRW: citations 3, 8].

4. **Layer 4 — Technical Defensibility**: conclusions supported by data, professional
   judgment extensions stated and justified, parameters appropriate for conditions. Assessed
   by TR and SME. Not automatable — requires engineering domain knowledge [GRW: citation 8;
   RA: citations 1-2].

### Six Defect Categories

5. **Technical Errors** (equivalent: bugs): calculation mistakes, incorrectly located
   boreholes, misinterpretation of field observations, missing information on drawings
   [RA: citation 8-13].

6. **Administrative Omissions** (equivalent: vulnerabilities): failure to warn of
   investigation limitations, poor record-keeping (cryptic undated notes), failure to
   communicate uncertainties, no written confirmation of verbal advice [RA: citations 14-20].

7. **Taboo Words** (equivalent: critical vulnerabilities — voids PLI): "all", "every",
   "always", "never", "ensure", "guarantee", "certify", "safe", "must", "zero", "final".
   These elevate standard of care from negligence to strict liability and are excluded from
   PLI policies [RA: citations 3-5, 21-22].

8. **Ambiguity Language** (equivalent: code smells): "adequate", "sufficient", "general
   compliance", "highest quality", "timely manner", "workmanlike", "supervise". In legal
   disputes, courts interpret these terms to the engineer's detriment [RA: citations 23-25].

9. **Unqualified Scope** (equivalent: structural defects): vague scope description allows
   courts to infer comprehensive investigation; no statement that boring logs indicate
   conditions only at borehole locations; no third-party reliance restriction [RA: citations
   26-30]. The Applicability section is Mandatory in NZ enterprise templates.

10. **Extraneous Padding** (equivalent: technical debt): excessive geological description
    that provides ammunition for DSC claims ("thinly bedded" used by contractor to claim
    for unexpected rock excavation); dense data reports issued after construction is underway
    [RA: citations 31-33].

### Four Formal QA Checklists (NZ/AU Enterprise Practice)

11. **"20 Checkpoints in 90 Seconds"**: 20 yes/no questions for Author self-review before
    TR. Covers presentation, logical flow, tone, spelling/grammar, measurements accuracy,
    fresh-eyes proofreading [GRW: citations 5-7].

12. **Stability Analysis Review Checklist**: Applied by Author (Analysed By) and TR (Check
    By). 10 sub-sections covering slope geometry, material parameters (c', φ', su, γ),
    groundwater conditions, surcharge/seismic loading, critical slip surface validation,
    interslice forces, m-alpha values >0.5 [GRW: citations 9-10].

13. **Drafting QA Signoff Form**: Applied to all technical drawings before external issue.
    Minimum 3-day lead time. Covers CAD standards, coordinate systems, cross-references,
    naming conventions, north indicator, layout scales [GRW: citations 11-18].

14. **Section B Review Checklist** (ultimate gate): PD must physically sign. Applied twice
    — DRAFT gate and FINAL gate. Draft: calculations checked by multiple methods, data tables
    accurate, technical review complete, drawings stamped, templates applied, filed correctly.
    Final: client comments incorporated, effect on technical interpretation checked, final
    technical review complete [GRW: citations 19-21].

### Standard Review Cycle

15. **Five-stage cycle**: (1) Author self-review → (2) TR technical review → (3) PD DRAFT
    authorisation → (4) Author + TR incorporate client comments → (5) PD FINAL authorisation.
    For high-risk/high-value projects: escalates through Discipline Director → Sector Director
    → MD → Board [GRW: audit-trail knowledge doc].

16. **Small firm compression**: In firms of 5-15 staff, cycle compresses to effectively
    single-signer (principal reviews and signs). In 20-50 staff firms, PD typically doubles
    as TR. This compression is a known quality risk [practitioner experience].

### GIR Quality Gate (Minimum Pass Criteria)

17. Structural: Applicability/Scope Limitation section present with four mandatory
    sub-clauses (third-party reliance restriction, inferred conditions caveat, temporal
    boundary, changed-conditions notification). Standard-of-care clause present. Technical
    review (TR) signed off. All drawings appropriately stamped. Correct naming convention
    and filing [GRW: Section B; RA; pre-review-rule-validation].

18. Language: No taboo/absolute words. No ambiguity traps. Scope limitation uses approved
    conditional wording patterns [RA: taboo words guidance].

19. Technical (human-only): Conclusions supported by data. Professional judgment extensions
    stated and justified. Parameters appropriate for soil/rock conditions [GRW: PM Handbook].

### GBR Quality Gate (Distinct, More Demanding)

20. GBR must be the ONLY interpretive document in the contract — including a GIR is a
    critical compliance failure because it introduces conflicting interpretations [GBR:
    citations 16-19].

21. Page-turning cross-reference review is mandatory before issue — GBR cross-checked against
    general conditions, technical specifications, drawings, and GDR [GBR: citations 20-21].

22. Baseline statements must use definitive verbs only: "is", "will", "are". Forbidden words:
    "may", "can", "might", "up to", "could", "should", "some", "few", "would" [GBR:
    citations 52-53].

23. No unquantified adjectives in baselines: "large", "significant", "local", "many", "minor"
    must be strictly quantified or defined [GBR: citations 54-55].

24. Six common GBR defects to flag: (a) soft baselines (range without contractual average),
    (b) thesis-style length >30-50 pages, (c) two-tier formatting (binding vs. non-binding),
    (d) specification repetition, (e) unmeasurable parameters, (f) unrealistic conservatism
    [GBR: citations 57-83].

### No Existing Quality Scoring Model

25. No formal quality scoring system or maturity model for geotechnical report quality exists
    in the industry. What exists is field-execution-oriented: BDA Drillers Audit (operative
    competency), FPS initiative (GI data quality for piling input), project-specific digital
    audits on major infrastructure. BS 5930 and EC7 are guidance standards, not scoring
    frameworks [GEM: citations 1-7].

### Portfolio-Level Monitoring — TR/PD Behaviour

26. PDs already perform portfolio-level quality monitoring. The GRW notebook documents: "PDs
    also review the projects in their portfolio and discuss performance, risks, and issues
    with PMs regularly." Complex projects use a "Quality Management Plan and Register" for
    tracking quality and status of deliverables [GRW: citations 10-11]. This is standard
    enterprise practice, not a hypothetical use case.

27. What a PD wants from a portfolio quality view (GRW-grounded): document quality and
    client-readiness assessment; confirmation that legislative requirements are met and legal
    caveats are included; survivability assessment ("will this report survive a professional
    peer review?") [GRW: citations 13-15]. PDs do NOT want an aggregate score — they want
    auditable, traceable findings.

28. What a TR wants from a portfolio quality view (GRW-grounded): evidence of methodology
    soundness, data quality, and technical defensibility; confirmation that "the right answer
    has been given to the right question" [GRW: citations 12-13].

29. Aggregate quality scores ("firm quality: 74%") will be dismissed by senior engineers as a
    black box. A PD who cannot trace a score to specific named findings will either ignore it
    or treat it as false assurance. The correct portfolio view surfaces lists — "reports with
    open HIGH findings", "projects with no TR sign-off", "engineers with recurring flag
    patterns" — not a single score. This is a hard design constraint, not a preference.

30. Trend over time ("are we improving?") is a valid portfolio metric when the rule set is
    fixed. Trend data is meaningless when rules are simultaneously being changed, because
    improving scores may only reflect rule relaxation.

### Industry Precedents for Portfolio-Level Quality Monitoring

31. **AECOM** built a Ground Investigation Deliverables Management System (ProjectWise +
    ArcGIS) that centralised high volumes of GI data and "significantly improved quality
    control" across UK and Asia portfolios. Reduced deliverable turnaround by a factor of
    three [GEM: citation 1].

32. **Arup** built **Fuse**, a modular digital platform unifying fragmented project data.
    **ProjectGPT** (AI module within Fuse) enables engineers to query unstructured project
    documents — retrieving design standards, historical study outcomes, and site constraints
    across the firm's portfolio using natural language [GEM: citations 2-3].

33. **Network Rail** maintains GDMS (Geotechnical & Drainage Management System) with an
    archive of nearly 20,000 geotechnical reports. Uses **Microsoft PowerBI** to generate
    monthly bespoke reports on asset condition and inspection state across the network
    [GEM: citations 4-5].

34. **Keltbray Piling** built KIPS (Keltbray Integrated Piling System) for central
    regulation of all piling data capture. Uses portfolio data to identify "what acceptable
    progress looks like", find problem soil areas, and correct inefficiencies. Saves minimum
    two hours per site engineer per day [GEM: citations 6-8].

35. **Equipe/Aecom** on the Lower Thames Crossing used digital audit data to identify "trends
    and significant issues, including gaps within driller and support staff knowledge."
    Discovery triggered targeted training workshops — a direct precedent for "which engineers
    need coaching" as a portfolio quality dimension [GEM: citation 9].

### Workflow Trigger Points for Quality Checks

36. The natural author self-review trigger is immediately after drafting — the "Edit, check
    and proofread" stage — before the document enters formal review. Authors are coached to
    complete the "20 Checkpoints in 90 Seconds" checklist at this point, just before TR
    handoff [GRW: citations 1-4].

37. The document is expected to be "bullet-proof in terms of grammar and good writing" before
    it reaches TR [GRW: citation 6]. The quality scan therefore belongs at the author
    self-review stage, not at the TR review stage.

38. A secondary, earlier trigger exists. Engineers are coached to "talk to your tech reviewer
    midway" through drafting [GRW: citation 5]. This represents an optional intermediate
    checkpoint where structural completeness checks are most valuable (before the report
    body is finalised).

39. Real-time passive scanning (ESLint-style, flagging as the author types) is NOT aligned
    with engineering writing practice. A deliberate triggered action ("Run Quality Check"
    button) is the correct interaction model. Always-on background scanning will be disabled
    or ignored. This is a hard design constraint for the Word task pane integration.

40. TRs do not operate inside the author's task pane. Portfolio and TR-level views require a
    separate surface (web dashboard or distinct task pane mode). Do not assume the TR
    triggers quality checks from the author view.

### AI False-Positive Triage — Problem and Trust Boundary

41. The false-positive problem is real. Taboo words (e.g., "ensure") appear hundreds of times
    legitimately in a standard geotechnical report — in method descriptions, in contractor
    instructions, in standard clause references. A word-match scanner without context triage
    generates enough false positives that engineers learn to dismiss all taboo-word flags,
    including genuine ones. This is a worse outcome than no scanner.

42. AI context triage is therefore necessary, not optional. An AI reading the flagged finding
    in surrounding paragraph and section context is analogous to how a TR reads: the TR does
    not flag every instance of "ensure" — they read the context and exercise judgment.

43. Engineer trust in AI triage is severity-dependent:
    - **Note-level findings** (no PLI consequence): engineers will accept AI context-judgment
      and dismiss without further review. Appropriate.
    - **Check-level findings** (possible PLI exposure): engineers will read AI reasoning and
      form their own view. AI reasoning is valuable; AI making the final call is not trusted.
    - **Fix-level findings** (definitive taboo word in recommendations or conclusions
      context): engineers will not accept AI autonomous resolution. Human eyes required.
      TR specifically must confirm the fix.

44. The safe design model: AI provides reasoning + recommended disposition, engineer confirms
    or overrides. "This 'ensure' appears in a contractor instruction section, not in your
    recommendations — low risk. Dismiss?" The engineer reads and decides. Never autonomous
    resolution for PLI-sensitive findings.

45. If the AI proposes a rewrite of a flagged sentence (e.g., replacing "ensure" with
    "confirm"), that output must pass through the author and TR before any change is applied
    to the document. AI-generated replacement text in a professional liability context must
    not be auto-applied. This is consistent with the boundary established in
    ai-workflow-expansion-boundaries fact 8: "Risk-language checking is safe and valuable
    where it flags wording for human review."

46. The Risk Assessment notebook did not directly address engineer trust in automated QA
    tools. Facts 41-45 are practitioner-grounded, not notebook-grounded.

### Five Portfolio-Level Quality Dimensions for NZ/AU Firms

47. **Dimension 1 — Linguistic/PLI risk compliance** (highest priority, highest financial
    consequence): Taboo word absence in recommendations, conclusions, and scope sections
    [RA: citations 3-5, 21-22]; Scope Limitation section present with all four mandatory
    sub-clauses (third-party reliance restriction, inferred conditions caveat, temporal
    boundary, changed-conditions notification) [GRW: citation 7; RA: citation 18];
    standard-of-care clause present.

48. **Dimension 2 — Structural completeness compliance**: Applicability/Scope Limitation
    section present and correctly placed [GRW: Mandatory template labelling]; factual/
    interpretive delineation maintained; conclusions traceable to body data (machine-
    checkable: conclusions section references earlier sections).

49. **Dimension 3 — Review sign-off compliance**: Section B Review Checklist completed and
    signed at both Draft gate and Final gate [GRW: citations 7-9]; TR sign-off recorded and
    dated; PD sign-off recorded and dated. This dimension reveals whether the quality
    *process* is being followed, not just whether the document looks correct. "Projects with
    no TR sign-off" is a concrete, actionable portfolio metric.

50. **Dimension 4 — Template and operational discipline**: Firm template version applied;
    naming convention correct; document filed in the Issued Documents folder; drawing stamps
    correct ("not for construction" where required) [GRW: template discipline requirements].
    GEM precedents (AECOM, Keltbray) treat operational discipline — provenance and version
    control — as a core quality dimension, not cosmetic.

51. **Dimension 5 — Standards application signalling**: Are relevant NZ/AU standards cited
    where expected? (NZS 4431, AS 1726, NZGS guidelines, IDS). GEM evidence: 40% of
    geotechnical specialists lacked a good working knowledge of Eurocode 7 (EC7); 30% were
    failing to apply it [GEM: citations 15-16]. A portfolio view showing "reports with design
    parameters but no standard citation" is a legitimate coaching signal — not a compliance
    verdict. Absence of citation ≠ non-compliance, but it warrants review.

52. **Layer 4 (Technical Defensibility) must be explicitly excluded from the portfolio
    dashboard.** Assessing whether conclusions are supported by data, professional judgment
    extensions are justified, or parameters are appropriate for conditions requires
    engineering knowledge equal to or above the author [GRW: citation 2]. Claiming to
    assess this automatically will destroy credibility with senior engineers. The correct
    label in the dashboard: "Technical defensibility — not automatically assessed."

## Standards Referenced

- BS 5930:2015+A1:2020 (Code of practice for ground investigation) — best practice guidance,
  not a scoring framework [GEM: citations 5, 7]
- Eurocode 7 (EC7) — geotechnical design standard; second-generation Part 2 being restructured

  parameters, assumptions and raw data" [Engineering Standards]
