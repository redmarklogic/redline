# CEO Priorities in Geotechnical Engineering Firms

**Sub-domain**: contracts-and-risk
**Last verified**: 2026-04-30
**Confidence**: cross-referenced
**Sources**: Professional Services Firm Management notebook, Risk Assessment in Engineering notebook, Redline Founder Memos notebook

## Summary

The literature on professional services firm (PSF) management identifies three overarching goals that every engineering firm CEO must balance: outstanding client service, fulfilling careers for staff, and financial success. In practice, for a geotechnical consultancy, the top-three CEO priorities distil to: (1) financial performance — utilisation, multiplier, and cash flow; (2) talent — recruiting, retaining, and leveraging staff; and (3) liability protection — managing professional indemnity insurance and preventing claims through quality systems.

## Key Facts

1. Every PSF operates under a three-pronged mission: "To deliver outstanding client service; to provide fulfilling careers and professional satisfaction for our people; and to achieve financial success so that we can reward ourselves and grow." [PSF Management, citation 1]
2. "Profit is the only measure of successful design." CEOs focus on bottom-line financial performance including budgets, prices, contract terms, and financial results. [PSF Management, citation 2]
3. Utilisation ratio (proportion of chargeable staff time vs total time) is the primary operational metric. When it drops below ~62.5%, profitability becomes difficult. [PSF Management, citation 4]
4. Net effective multiplier (multiple of direct labour salary creating the billable rate) must be tracked monthly on every project. [PSF Management, citation 5]
5. Accounts receivable turnover averages ~65 days in design firms. Cash flow management is a major CEO concern. [PSF Management, citation 7]
6. The "people crisis": scarcity, high cost, and difficulty of retaining mid-level professionals. People join firms for careers, not jobs. [PSF Management, citations 11, 12]
7. Leverage (ratio of junior to senior staff) drives profitability. Systemic underdelegation — where senior staff do junior work — destroys profit, demotivates juniors, and traps seniors away from business development. [PSF Management, citations 15-20]
8. Client retention is cheaper than acquisition. Firms target clients, not projects. Clients buy trust and confidence, not technical expertise per se. [PSF Management, citations 21-24]
9. Professional liability insurance (PLI) functions as "catastrophe insurance" — premiums are a significant portion of annual overhead. Even minor disputes cost at least $10,000 from profits. [Risk Assessment, citations 2-4]
10. Insurers evaluate quality control procedures when setting premiums. Strict QC and professional development requirements are "beneficial to risk management." [Risk Assessment, citation 9]
11. Report review is "an important defence against professional liability claims." Junior engineers need structured review systems. [Risk Assessment, citations 5-7]
