# Practice of Engineering vs. Business of Engineering

**Sub-domain**: contracts-and-risk
**Last verified**: 2026-05-03
**Confidence**: cross-referenced
**Sources**: Risk Assessment in Engineering notebook, Geotechnical Report Workflows notebook, Redline Founder Memos notebook, CEO Priorities knowledge document

## Summary

Engineering firms operate on two distinct layers: the "practice of engineering" (physical equations, engineering theory, calculation checks) and the "business of engineering" (standards compliance, liability protection, insurance, contractual risk allocation). These layers are managed by different roles, catch different categories of errors, and serve different commercial purposes. A design can be physically sound but still expose the firm to negligence claims if it does not comply with applicable standards — because "failure to conform to standards generally accepted in the profession is strong evidence of negligence" in court.

## Key Facts

1. The standard of care (the legal benchmark for whether an engineer was negligent) is "measured by applicable professional standards of the engineering profession at the time the services were performed." [Risk Assessment, citation 1]
2. "Failure to conform to standards generally accepted in the profession is strong evidence of negligence." [Risk Assessment, citation 6]
3. A design that works physically but does not comply with standards means the firm "is not insured and you're exposing yourself to liability and you know maybe even criminal charges." [Founder Memos, citation 2]
4. In geotechnical practice, calculations checking and standards compliance checking are strictly separate review steps performed by different people under different mandates. Calculations are checked by a peer engineer ("check inputs, arithmetic, parallel simplified calculation"). Standards compliance is handled under a Regulatory Peer Review or Compliance Review, culminating in a Producer Statement (PS2). [Report Workflows, Table 5.1]
5. The Project Director (PD) conducts an "Oversight Review" that does not check the maths — it checks that client needs are met, legislative requirements are fulfilled, and the firm's reputation is preserved. This is a business protection function. [Report Workflows, citation 4]
6. Before work starts, firms perform "Go/No Go" decision gates checking the client's financial reputation, conflicts of interest, and whether the firm's PI insurance actually covers the proposed work. [Report Workflows, citations 12-15]
7. The firm's "Six Key Business Rules" require a commercially acceptable documented contract with a liability cap before any work can proceed. [Report Workflows, citations 18-20]
8. Applicability and limitation statements — disclaiming third-party reliance and explicitly stating the report is for "exclusive use of our client" — are mandatory business risk protection measures, not engineering checks. [Report Workflows, citations 7-9]
9. Insurers evaluate quality control procedures when setting PI insurance premiums. Strict QC and professional development are "beneficial to risk management." [Risk Assessment / CEO Priorities, citation 9-10]
10. Report review is "an important defence against professional liability claims." [Risk Assessment / CEO Priorities, citations 5-7]
