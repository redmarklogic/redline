# Bespoke Contract Clause Risks — NZ Geotechnical Practice

**Sub-domain**: contracts-and-risk
**Last verified**: 2026-05-14
**Confidence**: practitioner-grounded
**Sources**: 25+ years NZ consulting practice; cross-reference: Geotechnical Report Workflows notebook (Six Key Business Rules, Go/No-Go gate), Risk Assessment in Engineering notebook (standard of care, PI insurance)

## Summary

In NZ geotechnical practice, "bespoke conditions" means client-written contracts that replace or modify standard industry templates (ACENZ Short Form Conditions of Engagement, CCCS). These contracts are the leading commercial-layer cause of PI (Professional Indemnity) insurance exposure in small and mid-size firms. The CAS (Consulting Engineers Advancement Society) QA renewal question was introduced specifically to drive firms toward standard conditions and away from signing bespoke contracts without scrutiny.

## Key Facts

1. Standard NZ consulting conditions (ACENZ Short Form, CCCS) were written by the profession to protect engineers. Client-written bespoke contracts were written by clients (and their lawyers) to protect clients. These are not equivalent documents. [Practitioner assessment]

2. **Fitness for purpose** is the highest-risk clause type. It converts a duty of reasonable skill and care into an absolute warranty. If the ground performs outside the prediction — even due to natural variability, not negligence — the engineer is liable. PI insurance covers negligence, not breach of warranty. A fitness for purpose clause can void PI cover entirely on the affected claim. [Practitioner assessment; consistent with Risk Assessment notebook on standard of care and PI policy scope]

3. **Removal of the liability cap** is the second most common failure. ACENZ standard conditions cap the engineer's total liability, typically at a multiple of the professional fee. Client-drafted contracts frequently delete this cap or replace it with "contract value of the project." A $12K site investigation and report on a $4M residential project can create $4M of uninsured exposure. [Practitioner assessment]

4. **Unrestricted third-party reliance** removes the standard disclaimer that the report is for the exclusive use of the named client and stated purpose. Financiers, future purchasers, and contractors can then rely on the report as a warranty and sue directly in tort. The engineer never vetted these parties, was never paid by them, and cannot know how the report will be used. [Practitioner assessment; consistent with Report Workflows notebook on applicability/limitation statements as mandatory risk protection]

5. **Inclusion of consequential loss** removes the standard exclusion for indirect losses. A contractor's delay and disruption costs on a project can easily exceed the total geotechnical fee by a factor of 10–100. [Practitioner assessment]

6. **Novation without consent** allows the client to assign the contract to a third party without the engineer's agreement. The engineer inherits obligations to an unknown party whose project may be materially different from the original brief. [Practitioner assessment]

7. **Design-build back-to-back risk bridging** is the most dangerous construction-project variant. The main contractor holds design-build liability and back-to-backs it to the geotechnical subcontractor. The engineer ends up carrying contractor-style risk (remediation costs, delay damages, consequential losses) on a consultant-style fee — a category of liability that PI insurance does not cover. [Practitioner assessment]

8. The NZ large-firm Six Key Business Rules require "a commercially acceptable documented contract with a liability cap before any work can proceed." This is an internal commercial gate, not a regulatory requirement — small firms rarely have equivalent formal controls. [Geotechnical Report Workflows, citations 18-20]

## Clause Risk Ranking (practitioner ranking, NZ context)

| Rank | Clause type | Why dangerous |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Fitness for purpose | Voids PI cover; no negligence required for liability |
| 2 | Removal of liability cap | Uncapped exposure vs. small fee |
| 3 | Unrestricted third-party reliance | Unknown claimants, unlimited class |
| 4 | Consequential loss inclusion | Delay costs dwarf professional fees |
| 5 | Novation/assignment without consent | Unknown client, unknown project |
| 6 | Design-build back-to-back risk | Contractor-class liability, PI does not cover |

## Product Implication (for Redline team)

A contract clause review tool — scanning for deviations from ACENZ/CCCS standard wording and flagging risky clauses — is technically feasible (NLP-based clause comparison against standard templates). It would be professionally valuable in the NZ market, particularly for small firms that lack in-house legal review.

However, this is a distinct product from Redline's current report pre-review function:
- Gate: Engagement (pre-contract signing) vs. report submission (pre-sign-off)
- User: Principal/PM reviewing contracts vs. engineer/reviewer checking report content
- Knowledge domain: NZ professional services contract law vs. geotechnical engineering
- Comparison baseline: ACENZ Short Form/CCCS wording vs. technical checklist

This is a strategic product direction question for Ron and Mark, not a domain feasibility question. From a domain perspective: the professional need is real and the market gap is genuine. Small firms are the most exposed and the least equipped to catch these clauses.
