## Context: why most of this talk does NOT transfer

Koi did many impressive things, but two inputs made them possible — and Redline has neither:

1. **Venture-capital money.** Koi had raised large investor funding, which paid for big
   marketing stunts and a team of specialists (separate sales and customer-success teams).
2. **A built-in urgency machine.** Koi sells *cybersecurity*. That industry produces a
   constant stream of newly disclosed security flaws ("CVEs" — Common Vulnerabilities and
   Exposures, publicly catalogued software security holes). Each one scares companies into
   buying *now*. Koi did not have to create that fear; the ecosystem generates it for free.
   **Geotechnical engineering has no equivalent.**

**Segment mismatch:** Koi sold six-figure deals to large US enterprises. Redline sells a
**<$100 product to small and medium businesses (SMBs)**, deliberately avoiding long
enterprise sales cycles, and plans a gradual rollout of **10 partner customers → 100 → 1000**
to protect reputation while polishing the product.

Strip out the budget and the free fear-machine, and only a narrow — but real — kernel transfers.

## Ron — Strategy & GTM

### 1. Proof-of-Value (POV) as a founder-discipline tool *(highest-value takeaway)*

A POV is a short, time-limited trial where the customer proves the product genuinely helps,
beyond a sales demo. The founder explicitly wants this as a focus/discipline mechanism.

- **Hard deadline (~14 days), no extensions.** Koi used 21 days and refused to move it. The
  deadline is a leash on the *founder's* time, not the customer's — open-ended onboarding lets
  one customer consume unlimited founder hours.
- **One use-case, "well within capability."** Prove one thing the rough early product does
  well; don't promise everything.
- **Write the acceptance criterion before day 1, in value terms not features.** e.g. "By day
  14, partner used the core job N times on real work and reports it saved time / avoided error."
  This is the keep/kill test.
- **Day-7 "summation" checkpoint** (20–30 min): does the core job work, what broke, on track?
  Acts as an early warning for product bugs before wider rollout.
- **Day-14 = decision, not shut-off.** Force a clear yes/no ("paying customer, or not a fit?").

**DO NOT COPY:** Koi switched the product *off* at trial end to force urgency. In the partner
phase this would break the most fragile asset (reputation) and punish partners who gave
feedback. Access-expiry is legitimate later, at self-serve scale.

### 2. Pricing

- **Never free — even the 10 partners pay something** (discounted founding-partner price).
  Free pilots yield polite feedback; paid pilots yield honest feedback ("skin in the game").
- **Discount in year one because product is in build + they give feedback — but not free.**
- **Price openly, honestly, up front.**
- **DO NOT COPY:** anchor-high price haggling ("start at half a million") and
  procurement-department theatre — irrelevant at a two-digit price.

### 3. Customer journey for the 10-partner phase

- **"Separation of church and state" (separate sell vs. enable teams) does NOT apply** — that
  solves a two-team-org problem Redline doesn't have. But separate the *moments, not the
  people*: enablement on weekly working calls (no money talk); convert/no-convert as a separate
  scheduled day-14 call. Same head, different hats.
- **Transferable & cheap:** shared channel per partner + weekly 30–45 min call (don't go dark
  and reconvene at the end); pre-meeting touch; **always book the next touch in the room.**
- **Time budget:** ~45 min/partner/week × 10 ≈ 7.5 hrs/week; POV discipline caps the ballooning.

### 4. Run the process

- **Transferable core:** *you run the process or it runs you.* Own the narrative of the partner
  journey; don't let partners improvise an open-ended evaluation.
- **DOES NOT transfer:** multi-threading up an org chart, 2→20 people in the room, per-persona
  buying-committee value matrix. SMB buyer = a committee of one.
- **Design the exchange legibly:** discounted founding-partner price in return for a clear day-14
  usage criterion + weekly call + honest feedback. Make their win real for *their* work, not
  disguised QA testing.

### 5. Ron — do NOT copy

Six-figure anchoring · procurement theatre · access shut-off in partner phase · separate
Sales/CS org · org-climbing + persona matrix · brand theatrics · AI-enriched account-based
outbound (unit economics fail at <$100).

**Non-obvious thing that DOES transfer:** the "I don't know" honesty + no-bullshit feedback
culture. Founder honesty about what's crude *is* the trust mechanism that yields real feedback.

## John — Marketing

### 1. Free-tool-as-buying-signal

- Koi's free browser-extension risk-scorer was useful standalone, silently captured behavioural
  intent ("person at company X scanned 3 risky extensions"), and fed fast outbound.
- **Pattern transfers, economics mostly don't** — a six-figure deal funds a giveaway; <$100 ACV
  (Annual Contract Value) doesn't. Bar: cheap to build, high-quality signal, no cannibalisation.
- **Candidate analogue (INFERENCE — Graeme must confirm credibility):** a free single
  high-frequency geotech calculation, in-browser; the signal is *repeated in-context use* (same
  user 5× in a week → live project → in-market now), mirroring Koi's signal.
- **Verdict: DEFER past Phase 1.** With 10 hand-picked partners you already know who they are.

### 2. Thought-leadership / the geotech "CVE" question

- **Least transferable part.** Cyber runs on dated, fear-driven CVE disclosures the market
  refreshes for free; geotech runs on correctness, standards, liability. Don't force it.
- **Realistic equivalent (Graeme owns substance):** standards/code changes are the closest
  dated-authoritative hook (but periodic — you don't control the tap). Use "They Ask You Answer"
  / Big-5 (openly answer pricing, problems, comparisons, reviews) — realistic for one person.
- **Re-engagement tactic IS transferable & cheap:** every genuinely-new publish → short note to
  dormant leads. Consistency reactivates cold leads — but one weak send burns trust in a small
  expert audience.
- **Verdict:** Koi-scale engine NO; lightweight Big-5 + standards commentary YES, but long-runway.

### 3. Social selling / LinkedIn + pre-meeting touch *(DO THIS NOW — lowest cost, highest leverage)*

- **Steal verbatim:** pre-meeting email — "Looking forward to Monday; we'll cover 1-2-3, anything
  specific you want me to focus on?"
- **LinkedIn connect before each call** (second channel + persistent handle). Add all 10 partners
  and relevant colleagues.
- **Founder builds an industry persona** — *easier* than for Amit: small pond, real domain
  practitioner, engineers trust engineers. Best near-term channel.
- **All 10 partners = strategic accounts, white-glove each.** Tiering only matters at 100→1000.
- **Posting:** 10:1 give-to-ask ratio.

### 4. Brand / X-factor on ~zero budget

- **Principle holds** (technical buyers are human; polish builds memory/trust). **Cyber tactics
  don't** (no RSA conference to ambush, no swag budget, "anti-cyber cool" is meaningless to a
  civil engineer).
- **Inverted insight:** in geotech the noise is *dated, clunky, hard-to-trust software.* Your
  X-factor is **credibility and clarity, not cool.**
- **Cheap "stunt" = radical transparency:** publish the <$100 price openly while the category
  hides behind "contact sales." Free differentiator + memory hook.
- **Design $ worth it:** product UI, pricing page, the 1–2 assets a prospect touches pre-buy. At
  <$100 self-serve, the product *is* the marketing. **Vanity:** conference theatrics, swag,
  animated decks, logo refresh. *(Product/onboarding polish = Matt's lane.)*

### 5. John — do NOT copy

Free data-product as near-term build · research-lab content engine / "control the CVE tap" ·
"spongy-AI" automated outbound (Clay + auto-add + auto-message + auto-call — burns reputation in
a small tight-knit geotech community) · RSA theatrics/swag/animated decks · timing pushes to VC
fundraising quarters.

## Joint synthesis — the transferable kernel

| Do now (Phase-1, near-zero cost) | Owner | Defer to 100→1000 |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Define 14-day POV: one use-case, value-based acceptance criterion, day-7 summation, day-14 decision | Founder → frame w/ Mark | Access-expiry urgency mechanic |
| Founding-partner pricing: discounted, **never free** | Founder + Ron | Free tier / PLG acquisition |
| Weekly 30-min call + shared channel + book-next-touch-in-room | Founder | Sales/CS team split |
| Pre-meeting email + LinkedIn connect ritual | Founder + John | AI/Clay outbound machine |
| Founder LinkedIn persona, 10:1 give-to-ask | Founder + John | Big-5 content library, free signal tool |
| Transparency as differentiator (publish the price) | Founder + John | Standards-change content engine |
| Polish product UI + pricing page only | Matt | Brand stunts / swag |

**Meta-lesson:** the POV is not a sales gimmick — it's the operating discipline the founder
asked for. Time-box + value-defined acceptance criterion + honest feedback-for-discount exchange
keeps the founder focused and protects reputation across the cohort gate.

## Recommended reading

Note: these are advisor recommendations from memory — confirm exact titles before relying on them.

- **The Mom Test — Rob Fitzpatrick.** Extracting *honest* feedback from early customers. Most
  on-point for the 10-partner phase.
- **Monetizing Innovation — Ramanujam & Tacke.** Price-before-product, willingness-to-pay
  discipline, scaled to a <$100 SMB buyer.
- **Crossing the Chasm — Geoffrey Moore.** Early believers → mainstream without the wheels coming
  off. Strategic frame for the gated 10→100→1000 rollout.
- **They Ask, You Answer — Marcus Sheridan.** Transparency-as-marketing for a low-budget niche.
- **Obviously Awesome — April Dunford.** Stand out by being unmistakably right for geotech, not
  the loudest.
