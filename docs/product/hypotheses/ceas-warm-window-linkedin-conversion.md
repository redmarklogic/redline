## Hypothesis Statement

If John contacts Tier 1 NZ civil engineering firm principals from the CEAS/Aon webinar
participant list via LinkedIn within 6 weeks of the 14 May 2026 webinar (by 25 June 2026),
then the connection acceptance rate and positive response rate will be materially higher
than an equivalent cold list of NZ civil engineering principals who did not attend the webinar,
because the shared CEAS/Aon insurance context creates a warm common reference, reduces
cold-start friction, and the webinar topic (PI insurance and AI risk in engineering reports)
is directly adjacent to Redline's positioning.

---

## What Would Disprove This

- Connection acceptance rate from the CEAS list is not materially higher than a cold control
  list of equivalent NZ civil engineering principals.
- Positive response rate (open question, booked call, or meaningful reply) from the CEAS list
  is not materially higher than the cold control within 2 weeks of connection.

---

## Metrics

| Metric | CEAS list target | Cold baseline expectation |
|---|---|---|
| Connection acceptance rate | > 40% | ~20–25% (B2B LinkedIn cold) |
| Positive response rate (within 2 weeks of connection) | > 20% | ~5–10% (B2B LinkedIn cold) |

This is a small-n experiment (10–15 Tier 1 contacts vs. a cold control of similar size).
Not statistically rigorous at this scale — directionally useful for Phase 1 GTM calibration.

---

## Current Assumption State

Not yet tested. Grounded in three signals:

1. The webinar created a shared context event. A warm reference exists that John can
   reference credibly in a connection request without it being a cold unsolicited pitch.
2. The webinar subject (PI insurance, AI risk in engineering reports) is directly adjacent
   to Redline's positioning: "the audit trail your insurer will ask for." The topic framing
   is already doing pre-sell work on the list.
3. Warm window theory: B2B outreach that references a recent shared event converts higher
   than cold outreach to the same audience. This is a standard B2B sales principle —
   not yet measured in the NZ engineering market specifically.
