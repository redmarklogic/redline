## ICP (Ideal Customer Profile)

**Primary**: Senior and Principal Geotechnical Engineers at NZ consultancies with 5–50 staff.

This is Perrie's reviewer — the person whose trust unlocks firm-level adoption. Perrie is the Day-1 user (the junior-to-mid engineer producing the report). The senior is the adoption unlock (the person who decides whether to trust an AI quality layer in their review workflow, and whose name is on the PI insurance).

**Secondary**: Firm directors at those same consultancies.

The secondary ICP is thinking about PI insurance implications, regulatory exposure, and whether they can tell clients they have a quality-assurance layer in place. They are reachable through the same content as the primary ICP but respond to slightly different framing (risk management, not workflow efficiency).

## Content Strategy

### Step 2 — Website live, skeleton generator not yet demo-able

**Cadence**: 2 original posts per week + daily 10:1 LCS ritual (see below).

**Topic priority order:**

1. **NZ standards analysis** — one standard per post, one version-gap per post, one implication for practitioners. Example: NZS 4431 superseded in 2022, what changed, why a report citing the 1989 edition is exposed in a post-event review. These posts are the launch narrative made tangible. They demonstrate that Redline is grounded in the practitioner's world, not in generic AI capability.

2. **Dispute pattern education** — publicly reported NZ geotechnical disputes or Indemnity Matters case studies (anonymised, practitioner-framed — not legal advice). What happens when a report fails quality in a post-event review. The audience is the senior who has seen a dispute up close or who fears being the one to miss something. These posts create emotional resonance for the problem Redline solves.

3. **Cognitive cost angle** — the senior review bottleneck, the cost of being the reviewer at a 10-person firm where the director is also billing. The framing is not "AI saves time" — it is "the review is the firm's quality guarantee, and right now that guarantee depends entirely on whether the reviewer is fresh or tired." This angle speaks to the secondary ICP (firm directors) as much as the primary.

**CTA**: Waitlist URL on every post. No exceptions. No "follow for updates" or "like for more" phase.

**Redline branding**: The founder's profile headline and About section reference Redline. Original posts do not pitch the product — they build the problem narrative. The waitlist URL is the only product reference in post content.

### Step 3 — Skeleton generator demo-able

**Cadence**: 3 original posts per week + daily 10:1 LCS ritual (unchanged).

**Added topic type**: Product demonstrations — screen recordings or screenshot sequences showing the skeleton generator at work on a real NZ report type (GBR, GIR). These posts shift from "here is the problem" to "here is one answer to part of the problem." Demonstration posts should include a domain-accurate caption explaining what the skeleton generator checks and why the structure it produces is grounded in NZ standards.

### Step 4 — Full media wing

**Cadence**: 4–5 original posts per week + daily 10:1 LCS + Dream 100 direct outreach motion.

Dream 100 targeting mechanics are deferred to a separate planning session.

## The 10:1 LCS Rule — Daily Ritual

**Rule**: For every 1 original post published, leave 10 thoughtful comments on other practitioners' posts.

**In practice for NZ geotechnical LinkedIn** (the market is thin — approximately 300–800 relevant practitioners total):

| Source pool | Daily target | Who to follow |
|---|---|---|
| NZ/AU geotech practitioners | 5 comments/day | GNS Science researchers posting on liquefaction/ground hazard; EQC-affiliated engineers posting on Christchurch-era lessons; NZGS working group members posting on standards consultations; CAS-adjacent practitioners |
| UK/global geotech supplement | 5 comments/day | BGA (British Geotechnical Association) community; UK ground engineers; international practitioners posting on standards, dispute patterns, or AI-in-engineering topics |
| **Total** | **10 comments/day** | |

**Why the UK supplement is legitimate**: NZ practitioners are on LinkedIn and they see activity in adjacent professional communities. The algorithm surfaces "your network commented on this" across geography — a comment on a UK BGA post is visible to the founder's NZ geotech connections. The professional problems are structurally identical. UK geotech content is significantly more active and provides enough daily volume to sustain the 10:1 rule without depleting the NZ pool.

---

## Comment Quality Standard

A qualifying comment adds exactly one of three things:

1. **A NZ-specific example or case reference not in the original post.** ("In the Hawke's Bay context, the challenge you're describing is compounded by the GNS hazard map version question — we saw this in [publicly known context].") This signals genuine domain knowledge and NZ-specific grounding.

2. **A reframing question that surfaces a genuine nuance.** ("The structure-versus-content distinction you're making here — does it hold when the scope is a Stage 1 GBR with a regulatory deadline attached?") This demonstrates that the founder has thought beyond the surface of the question.

3. **A relevant NZ/NZGS standards reference the original poster did not name.** ("Worth noting that NZGS Module 5 revised this guidance in 2024 — the pre-2024 version had a different requirement on this exact point.") This builds authority in exactly the domain Redline occupies.

**Minimum length**: Two sentences.
**Never**: Pitches Redline. Says "Great point!" Asks for a follow. Links to the waitlist. Comments are purely for authority-building and professional relationship initiation.
**Test**: Would the person receiving this comment want to respond to it? If yes, it is a qualifying comment.

## Founder LinkedIn Profile — PIPA Framework

The founder's LinkedIn profile About section follows the PIPA framework (Pain, Insight, Position, Action):

- **Pain**: Name the problem NZ geotech seniors and directors are living with — wrong standard versions, review bottlenecks, PI exposure from AI-assisted reports that nobody systematically checked.
- **Insight**: State the non-obvious observation — the problem is not that engineers make mistakes; the problem is that the quality-assurance layer is informal, person-dependent, and invisible to insurers.
- **Position**: State what Redline is and who it is for — a pre-review quality layer for geotechnical reports, grounded in NZ standards (NZS, NZGS), built for the reviewer not the drafter.
- **Action**: Waitlist URL. One sentence. No soft-sell.

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

- Automated commenting tools — destroy credibility instantly in a small professional market.
- Commenting on every post from the same person in a short window — reads as surveillance.
- Pitching Redline in the comments of competitors' posts.
- Posts that begin with "AI is changing everything in engineering" — generic, low-authority, will not reach the ICP.
- "Follow for more" phases — every post must have a direct CTA to the waitlist URL.
- Posting volume that outpaces the founder's genuine domain knowledge — the authority signal degrades if posts become thin or repetitive.
