### Article 1 — Publish First

**Working title**: "What is your senior engineer actually for?"

**Big 5 category**: Best of (reframe — "here's the right job, and it's not what
you're spending the time on")

**Target reader**: Technical Director or QA lead who has felt the pain of a senior
engineer being tied up in documentation checking before a report can go out.

**Core argument**:

A senior engineer's value is in engineering judgment: catching the wrong methodology,
the unsupported conclusion, the parameter that contradicts the borehole log. It is not
in verifying that NZS 4431 is cited correctly, that the standard number is not
transposed, or that a clause is present. That is a pattern-matching and compliance
task. Using a senior engineer for it is the most expensive way to perform it.

Approximately half of a senior engineer's current review time (this is a practitioner
estimate, not a published figure) is consumed by compliance mechanics — citation
accuracy, clause presence, forbidden language checking, standard number verification.
The other half is engineering judgment. This article asks: what if the first half were
handled by a tool designed for it?

**Why this article is most valuable**:

It reframes the conversation from "AI review assistant" to "what senior engineers
should be doing." It is not a product pitch. It is a strategic argument the audience
will find themselves agreeing with. It opens naturally to Redline without requiring a
hard product mention.

**Publication order**: First.

**Graeme review gate**: Required before external publication — specifically for the
55/45 practitioner estimate and for any specific engineering examples used.

### Article 2

**Working title**: "Two types of review failure — and only one of them Redline fixes"

**Big 5 category**: Problems (honest differentiation — naming what the tool does not do
is more credible than naming only what it does)

**Target reader**: Senior engineer or principal who has heard AI-in-engineering
warnings and is trying to calibrate actual risk.

**Core argument**:

There are two distinct failure modes in AI-assisted report production.

**Case 1**: Plausible citations, wrong engineering underneath. The methodology is
unsound; the conclusion is not supported by the data; the parameter choices are
optimistic. The AI writes confidently and the document looks clean. This failure mode
is rare, catastrophic when it occurs, and not preventable by Redline. It is preventable
only by a senior engineer who knows the site, the method, and the geology.

**Case 2**: Sound engineering, wrong documentation. The methodology is correct; the
conclusions are defensible; but a standard number is transposed, a required clause is
absent, the language has drifted from hedged professional opinion to absolute statement.
This failure mode is frequent, everyday, and fully preventable by a compliance review
tool.

Redline addresses Case 2. It does not replace the judgment required for Case 1. Firms
that understand this distinction will use Redline correctly.

**Why this article is valuable**:

Honest positioning builds trust faster than capability claims. The audience is
sceptical of AI tools that overclaim. An article that clearly states what the tool
does not do — and why that limitation is rational — earns credibility with the
quality-committed buyer. It simultaneously disqualifies the throughput buyer who
wants a cheaper substitute for senior review.

**Publication order**: Second.

### Article 3

**Working title**: "The firm that does more reports with Redline — and the firm that
does better ones"

**Big 5 category**: Problems (buyer-type differentiation — invites the reader to
self-select)

**Target reader**: Principal evaluating whether Redline fits their practice. This
piece surfaces the values question before the procurement question.

**Core argument**:

Redline creates a choice. A compliance review that used to take ~2.2 hours takes
3 minutes. That time does not disappear — the firm decides what to do with it.

Quality-committed firms reinvest it: the senior engineer spends the freed time on
deeper methodology review, more thorough parameter checking, a harder look at the
conclusions. The report that goes out is more defensible, not faster.

Throughput firms pocket it: the senior engineer starts reviewing a second report. More
reports, same depth. The freed time generates more revenue but not more assurance.

These produce opposite outcomes for the firms' clients, their PI (Professional
Indemnity) insurers, and ultimately for Redline's reputation. Redline wants to work
with the first type of firm. This article is explicit about that.

**Why this article is valuable**:

The two-firm framing is a natural self-selection filter. Quality-committed buyers read
this and feel validated. Throughput buyers read this and self-identify as the wrong
fit — before they buy, not after. This article does the ICP (Ideal Customer Profile)
qualification work that would otherwise fall to sales.

**Publication order**: Third.

### Article 4

**Working title**: "Why the 3-minute compliance check is not the product — the
engineering time it returns is"

**Big 5 category**: Best of (value-reframe — the feature is not the point, the
outcome is)

**Target reader**: Senior engineer who has seen a demo and is thinking "this saves me
time on admin." This piece redirects attention to what to do with that time.

**Core argument**:

Redline's compliance check runs in about 3 minutes. The natural reaction is to frame
this as a time-saving tool. That framing is technically accurate and strategically
wrong.

The product is not the 3 minutes saved. The product is the 2.2 hours returned to
engineering judgment. The senior engineer who is no longer checking standard numbers
is now available to spend that time on the work only they can do: assessing whether
the recommended foundation design is right for this site, not whether the standard
number for the design code is typed correctly.

Time savings are the mechanism. Engineering quality is the outcome. Leading with
mechanism attracts the throughput buyer. Leading with outcome attracts the
quality-committed one.

**Why this article is valuable**:

It corrects the instinctive but wrong pitch — "Redline saves you time" — and replaces
it with the right one: "Redline returns your most expensive resource to the work that
justifies their cost." It is also directly useful for the sales team and for any
partner who might write or speak about Redline.

**Graeme review gate**: Required if the article uses the 55/45 split or any specific
engineering examples.

**Publication order**: Fourth.

### Article 5

**Working title**: "What does a senior engineer miss when they're checking citations
instead of conclusions?"

**Big 5 category**: Problems (cost-of-the-status-quo — the loss framing)

**Target reader**: Principal or QA lead who has never explicitly costed the attention
trade-off in their review workflow.

**Core argument**:

When a senior engineer spends the first half of a report review on compliance
mechanics — citation accuracy, standard numbers, clause presence, forbidden language —
they arrive at the engineering content with reduced cognitive budget. The depth of
the engineering review is a function of the time and attention available for it.

The question is not only "what does it cost to have a senior engineer do compliance
checking?" It is also: "what does the senior engineer not catch in the engineering
review because they spent the first two hours on documentation?"

This is the hidden cost of the current workflow. It is not visible in time sheets. It
is visible only when a defensible technical question surfaces in a claim or dispute
and the senior engineer cannot answer it because it was not the focus of the review.

**Why this article is valuable**:

It surfaces a cost the audience has not quantified. Most principals think about
efficiency (senior time spent on admin). Fewer have thought about the attention
trade-off — what engineering judgment is displaced by compliance mechanics. This
article makes that displacement tangible and uncomfortable.

**Graeme review gate**: Required. This article makes implicit claims about the depth
of engineering review being attention-limited. Graeme should confirm this is a
recognisable practitioner experience before it goes external.

**Publication order**: Fifth.

## Section C — One Thing Not to Do

**Do not lead with time savings as the headline.**

"Redline cuts your review time by 55%" is the throughput pitch. It is technically
coherent. It attracts firms whose primary goal is throughput — more reports in less
time with the same headcount. Those firms will use Redline to pocket the freed time,
not reinvest it. The outcome is lower-quality reports with a compliance check
on top. That is not the outcome Redline is built for, and it is not the reputation
Redline should build.

The headline that attracts the right buyer is: "What would your senior engineer
catch if they weren't checking citations?"
