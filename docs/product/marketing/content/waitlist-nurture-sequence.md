## Purpose

The website (Step 1) goes live before the skeleton generator is demo-able (Step 3). In that gap, waitlist signups lose brand familiarity and context. This sequence keeps the brand present, deepens the problem narrative, and — in Email 2 — plants the first soft co-development recruitment ask.

## Email 1 — "You're on the list. Here's why that matters."

**Trigger**: Automated. Fires within 48 hours of waitlist form submission.
**From**: Founder (personal email, not a company alias).
**Subject line**: "You're on the list. Here's why that matters."

**Content brief:**

Confirm the signup in one sentence. Do not make it feel like a transactional confirmation email.

Restate the problem in one paragraph: NZ geotech standards change — NZS editions are superseded, NZGS modules are revised, and the version a practitioner cites in a report may not be the version that was current at the time of the site investigation or, worse, at the time of a post-event review. The wrong-version error is predictable, not random — it is the result of an informal quality-assurance process that depends on whoever is reviewing the report knowing the current standard and remembering to check it.

Give one concrete example: NZS 4431:1989 was superseded by NZS 4431:2022. A report citing the 1989 edition, submitted after 2022, is citing a withdrawn standard — and if that report is examined in a PI dispute, the citation is a traceable gap. This is not a hypothetical. It is the class of error Redline is designed to catch before the report leaves the desk.

No product description. No ask.

Close: "We'll be in touch when beta access opens. In the meantime, this is the problem we're solving."

**Length target**: 200–280 words.

## Email 2 — "The standard error we see most often."

**Trigger**: 14 days after signup. *Mark must verify this timing aligns with the expected free-tier activation window. The co-development ask in this email should not land before the prospect has had a chance to use the skeleton generator. If activation data at Step 3 shows the average user takes more than 14 days to activate, this trigger should shift.*
**From**: Founder (personal email).
**Subject line**: "The standard error we see most often."

**Content brief:**

Open with a single, fully developed case study — anonymised and domain-accurate, not real, but realistic enough that a NZ geotech senior would recognise the scenario.

Structure of the case study:
1. Report type (e.g., Stage 1 GBR for a residential subdivision in a TC2/TC3 zone — you can name the zone type without naming a real site).
2. What the report cited (e.g., NZS 4431:1989).
3. What the correct standard was at the time of investigation (NZS 4431:2022).
4. The gap — specifically what the 2022 edition introduced that the 1989 edition did not cover (fill liquefaction susceptibility criteria, introduced post-Canterbury earthquake sequence).
5. What that gap meant for the engineer's position in a post-event review — not a legal claim, but a practitioner-level framing of the exposure.

This case study is not a horror story — it is a precise, educational walkthrough of one failure mode. The tone is the same as a good Indemnity Matters case write-up: clear, non-judgmental, focused on the pattern.

Close with a soft co-development ask: "If this pattern is familiar from your own experience, I'd value twenty minutes of your time to make sure I'm getting the details right before beta opens. Reply to this email and we'll find a time."

**This is the co-development recruitment instrument.** The ask is for a discovery conversation, framed as input to the product — not a sales conversation.

**Length target**: 350–450 words.

## Email 3 — "Beta access is opening."

**Trigger**: Manual trigger by founder when Step 3 (skeleton generator demo-able) is imminent. Alternative: fixed 28-day schedule from signup if manual trigger is not feasible in the email tool. The manual trigger is preferred — it allows the founder to open beta access in a controlled batch rather than on a rolling 28-day basis.
**From**: Founder (personal email).
**Subject line**: "Beta access is opening."

**Content brief:**

Name the product for the first time in the sequence: the Skeleton Generator. Two sentences on what it does — generates a structured outline for NZ geotechnical reports (GBR, GIR) grounded in current NZS and NZGS requirements, so the structure of the report is correct before the writing begins.

Describe the beta exchange: free access, works on real NZ report types, a feedback conversation with the founder as part of the arrangement. This is not a free trial with no obligation — it is a co-development exchange. The founder gets a practitioner's reaction; the practitioner gets early access and direct input into what the tool checks.

State the scarcity constraint honestly: "We're opening spots in the next two weeks. You're on the early access list — reply to this email to claim yours."

**No marketing copy, no design, no imagery required.** This email should read like a personal note from a founder to a professional peer.

**Length target**: 150–200 words.
