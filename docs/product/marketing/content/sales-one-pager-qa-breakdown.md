## The Problem

`[GRAEME: verify]` Geotechnical reports are the primary deliverable of every
geotechnical consultancy. They carry professional liability, inform construction
decisions, and represent the firm to clients, contractors, and regulators.

Most firms rely on a single senior reviewer to catch every error before the report goes
to the client. This reviewer -- typically a Principal or Technical Director -- is the
last line of defence.

## What Goes Wrong

`[GRAEME: verify]` Senior reviewers routinely catch errors that should never reach them:

- **Wrong standard references.** Reports citing superseded editions of AS, NZS, or BS
  standards, or standards from the wrong jurisdiction.
- **Copy-paste remnants.** Site names, client names, borehole numbers, or
  recommendations inherited from a previous project and not updated.
- **Missing sections.** Required sections omitted because the report was assembled from
  a previous job, not from a structured template.
- **Formatting inconsistencies.** Different heading styles, numbering conventions, and
  table formats within the same document, carried over from the source report.
- **Conclusions that do not match the data.** Interpretive summaries that contradict the
  factual data in the appendices, often because one was updated and the other was not.

These are not engineering judgement failures. They are process failures -- errors that a
structured workflow should prevent or catch upstream.

## Why It Persists

`[GRAEME: verify]` Three compounding factors:

1. **Digital QA checklists failed.** Most firms digitised their paper review forms
   between 2008 and 2015. Adoption dropped because the forms are disconnected from the
   report -- they ask the reviewer to verify things manually that the form itself cannot
   check.

2. **Templates are informal.** Unlike environmental or water engineering, geotechnical
   reports are typically assembled by copying the last similar project's report. There
   is no enforced template structure, so every report starts from a different baseline.

3. **Trust replaces process at scale.** Managers trust their experienced engineers and
   reduce oversight accordingly. This works for individuals but fails as a system --
   when volumes grow, the senior reviewer becomes a bottleneck, and errors get through.

## The Cost

`[GRAEME: verify]` The cost is measurable but rarely measured:

- **Senior engineer hours** spent on corrections that should have been caught upstream.
  Every hour a TD spends fixing headers and standard references is an hour not spent on
  engineering, business development, or mentoring.
- **Rework cycles** when errors are found late. Reports returned for correction delay
  project timelines and frustrate clients.
- **Professional liability exposure** when errors are not found at all. PI claims
  frequently trace back to errors that were present in the issued report -- errors a
  functioning QA process should have caught.

## The Opportunity

The firms that reduce reviewer burden will not do it by adding more checklists. They
will do it by embedding quality checks into the report production workflow -- catching
errors at the point of creation, not the point of final review.

This is not a new principle. Manufacturing calls it poka-yoke (mistake-proofing).
Software engineering calls it shift-left testing. The principle is the same: prevent
the defect, do not inspect for it.

`[GRAEME: verify]` Geotechnical engineering is ready for this shift. The tooling gap is
closing. The question for every firm is whether they move early or wait until their
competitors do.
