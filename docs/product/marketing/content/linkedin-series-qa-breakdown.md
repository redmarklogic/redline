## Post 1: The Reviewer's Time Burden

**Theme**: Senior reviewers spend disproportionate time on basic corrections.

If you are a Technical Director at a geotechnical consultancy, I have a question: how
did you spend your Monday morning?

`[GRAEME: verify]` If the answer involves correcting project numbers in headers,
chasing superseded standard references, fixing formatting from copy-pasted sections,
and flagging conclusions that do not match the data tables -- you are not alone.

This is not engineering review. This is error correction. And it is being done by the
most experienced, most expensive people in the firm.

The errors are not hard to spot. They are not subtle engineering judgement calls. They
are the kind of mistakes that a structured process should catch before the report ever
reaches a senior reviewer's desk.

`[GRAEME: verify]` The uncomfortable truth: in most firms, the senior reviewer IS the
QA process. Not because of a formal decision, but because everything else -- the
checklists, the intermediate reviews, the structured sign-offs -- has quietly eroded
under time pressure.

The question is not whether your reviewers are good enough. They are. The question is
whether it is a responsible use of their time.

## Post 2: The Digital Checklist That Nobody Uses

**Theme**: Digital checklists get low adoption because they are disconnected from
the work.

`[GRAEME: verify]` Around 2010, most mid-to-large consultancies digitised their QA
checklists. The paper review forms became Word documents, PDFs, or SharePoint entries.

The theory: better traceability, easier auditing, consistent compliance.

The reality: adoption dropped within a year or two. The form exists in the quality
management system. It might even be auditable for ISO purposes. But the people doing
the actual reviewing are not filling it in consistently.

Why? Because the checklist is disconnected from the work. The engineer writes the report
in Word. Then opens a separate form. Then answers questions about the report -- questions
the form cannot actually verify. "Have all standards been checked?" The form does not
know which standards are referenced, whether they are current, or whether they apply to
the jurisdiction. It takes the reviewer's word for it.

A form that asks a human to verify things a machine could verify is not quality
assurance. It is an administrative task that competes with actual engineering work for
the reviewer's time.

Every hour spent on a disconnected checklist is an hour not spent on engineering
judgement -- the thing only a senior reviewer can actually do.

## Post 3: "I Know My Team" Is Not a QA System

**Theme**: Trust replaces process as formal QA erodes.

"I trust my team."

"She is experienced -- I do not need to check her work line by line."

"He has done twenty of these reports."

`[GRAEME: verify]` These are real things that experienced engineering managers say.
And they are not wrong about the individual. The problem is that they are being used
as a substitute for a system.

Trust in a person is not the same as confidence in a process. A skilled engineer can
still produce a report with a reference to a superseded standard, or with a section
inherited from a different project, or with an outdated template. These are not
competence failures. They are the kind of errors that happen when the workflow does not
prevent them.

Quality management calls this "normalisation of deviance." When informal workarounds
produce acceptable results often enough, they become the accepted way of working. The
formal process still exists on paper. The actual process is: someone competent writes
it, someone senior reads it.

`[GRAEME: verify]` That works until the senior person is overloaded, on leave, or
reviewing their fifth report that afternoon. Then the gaps in the system become gaps
in the deliverable.

The answer is not to stop trusting people. It is to stop using trust as a replacement
for process.

## Post 4: Every Report Starts From the Last Job

**Theme**: Template discipline varies across engineering disciplines.

`[GRAEME: verify]` Environmental reports and water engineering reports, in many firms,
are produced from standardised templates. Consistent section structure. Consistent
formatting. Built-in checks. The reviewer's job is to assess the engineering content,
not hunt for missing sections and formatting errors.

Geotechnical reports are different. They are typically assembled by copying the last
similar project's report and modifying it. The "template" is whatever the last job
looked like.

This means every geotechnical report starts from a slightly different baseline. Different
formatting. Different section ordering. Different inherited content -- including content
from a different site, a different client, or a different jurisdiction.

`[GRAEME: verify]` The reviewer then has to check not just the engineering, but the
structure, the formatting, the leftover references, and the project-specific details
that were not updated. The review becomes a quality control exercise over the entire
document, not an engineering review of the technical content.

The disciplines that standardised their templates years ago removed an entire category
of reviewer burden. Their reviewers spend time on judgement calls. Geotechnical
reviewers spend time on formatting.

That is not a people problem. It is a tooling problem. And it is solvable.

## Post 5: Do Not Add Another Checklist -- Embed the Check

**Theme**: Embedded QA outperforms bolt-on review processes.

When quality problems surface, the instinct is always the same: add a checklist. Add a
review stage. Add a sign-off box.

Manufacturing solved this decades ago. The principle is called poka-yoke --
mistake-proofing. Do not rely on inspection to find defects. Design the process so that
defects cannot be introduced in the first place.

`[GRAEME: verify]` Applied to geotechnical report production: do not ask a reviewer to
verify that the correct standard edition is referenced. Make the reference automatic.
Do not ask a reviewer to check for missing sections. Enforce the section structure
through the template. Do not ask a reviewer to spot content carried over from the last
project. Flag it at the point of writing.

The difference between a checklist and embedded QA is timing. A checklist inspects the
output. Embedded QA prevents the error at the input. One requires senior engineer time.
The other does not.

`[GRAEME: verify]` Every form, every bolt-on review step, every manual sign-off that
asks a human to verify something a machine could verify -- that is time taken from
engineering. The firms that reduce reviewer burden will not do it by adding more forms.
They will do it by building the checks into the tools their engineers already use.

The review form nobody fills in is not the problem. It is a symptom. Fix the workflow,
and you will not need the form.
