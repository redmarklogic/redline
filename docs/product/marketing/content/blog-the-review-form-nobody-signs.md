Somewhere around 2010, most mid-to-large civil engineering consultancies started
digitising their QA processes. The paper review checklists -- the ones that lived in
ring binders behind someone's desk -- were scanned, converted to PDFs, or rebuilt as
Word forms. Some firms went further and put them into SharePoint or an intranet.

The intent was reasonable. Paper forms get lost. Paper forms are hard to audit. Paper
forms cannot be searched. Going digital was supposed to make QA traceable,
consistent, and enforceable.

It did not work. Not the way anyone expected.

## The checklist that nobody fills in

`[GRAEME: verify]` If you have worked in a geotechnical consultancy of any size, you
have seen the pattern. A digital review checklist is created. It might live in a
shared drive, a document management system, or an intranet page. It has fields for
the reviewer's name, the date, the report reference, and a series of tick boxes:
standards checked, calculations verified, figures cross-referenced, conclusions
consistent with data.

For the first few months, compliance is high. People fill it in because someone is
watching. Then a project runs hot. A deadline moves forward. A reviewer checks a
report on a Friday afternoon and sends it to the client on Monday morning. The
checklist does not get filled in. Nobody chases it. The next reviewer sees that
nobody chased it and draws the obvious conclusion.

`[GRAEME: verify]` Within a year or two, adoption drops. The checklist still exists.
It might still be referenced in the firm's quality management system. It might even
be auditable for ISO compliance. But the people doing the actual reviewing are not
using it consistently. The form is there. The process is not.

This is not unique to any one firm. It is the predictable outcome of layering a
digital form on top of a workflow that has not changed. The reviewer is still doing
the same thing they did with the paper form -- reading the report, marking it up,
sending it back. The only difference is that now they also have a form to fill in
afterwards. The form adds administrative overhead without changing the quality of
the review itself.

## The real QA system: one person's judgement

When the formal checklist fades, what replaces it? In most firms, the answer is a
senior reviewer. A Principal, a Technical Director, or a senior associate who reads
every report before it goes to the client.

`[GRAEME: verify]` This person is the de facto QA system. They catch formatting errors,
wrong standard references, inconsistencies between the borehole logs and the
interpretive sections, copy-paste remnants from the last project, and missing sections.
They catch the things that a checklist was supposed to catch -- but they catch them by
reading every page, not by ticking boxes.

This works. Until it does not.

The problem is not that these reviewers are bad at their job. The problem is that the
system depends entirely on them. If they are overloaded, errors get through. If they
are on leave, the backup reviewer may not catch the same things. If the firm grows and
the number of reports outpaces the number of senior reviewers, the bottleneck tightens.

`[GRAEME: verify]` Ask any Technical Director how they spend their Monday mornings. A
significant proportion will tell you they spend hours correcting basic errors in
reports that should never have reached their desk: wrong project numbers in headers,
references to superseded standards, conclusions that contradict the data tables,
formatting inconsistencies from copy-pasting across projects. These are not
engineering judgement calls. These are errors that a structured process should have
caught upstream.

## Trust replaces process

Here is where it gets uncomfortable. When formal QA processes fade and the workload
increases, firms start relying on something that feels like quality assurance but is
not: trust.

"I know my team." "She is a good engineer -- I do not need to check her work line by
line." "He has done twenty of these reports."

`[GRAEME: verify]` These are real statements that experienced engineers make, and they
are not wrong about the individual. The problem is that they are being used as a
substitute for a system. Trust in a person is not the same as confidence in a process.
A good engineer can still produce a report with the wrong standard referenced, or with
a section copied from a different site investigation, or with an outdated template.
These are not competence failures -- they are process failures.

The quality management literature calls this "normalization of deviance." When informal
workarounds produce acceptable results often enough, they become the accepted way of
working. The formal process still exists on paper, but the actual process is: a
competent person writes the report, and a senior person reads it. Everything in between
-- the checklists, the sign-offs, the structured review stages -- has been eroded.

`[GRAEME: verify]` This is not a failure of people. It is a failure of systems that
were designed for a paper-based world and never properly adapted for the way
engineers actually work with digital documents.

## The Swiss cheese problem

James Reason's Swiss Cheese Model, developed for safety-critical industries like
aviation and medicine, describes how accidents happen when holes in multiple defensive
layers align. Each layer -- training, checklists, supervision, automation -- has gaps.
Safety depends on the layers being independent enough that a gap in one is covered by
another.

`[GRAEME: verify]` In geotechnical report QA, there is often only one defensive layer:
the final reviewer. If the formal checklist is not being used consistently, and the
intermediate review stages have been compressed by time pressure, then the entire
quality defence rests on one person reading one document. If that person misses
something -- because they are rushed, because the error is subtle, because they are
reviewing their fifth report that day -- it goes to the client.

The professional liability implications are real. `[GRAEME: verify]` PI (Professional
Indemnity) insurance claims in geotechnical engineering often trace back to errors
that were present in the report at the time of issue -- errors that a structured review
process should have caught. The question insurers ask is not "was the engineer
competent?" but "was there a process that should have caught this, and did the process
function?"

When the honest answer is "the process existed on paper but the actual QA was one
person's judgement," the firm's position is weaker.

## Why checklists were always the wrong answer

The instinct, when faced with quality problems, is to add another checklist. Another
form. Another review stage. Another sign-off box. This instinct is understandable and
almost always counterproductive.

`[GRAEME: verify]` The reason digital checklists fail in practice is not that engineers
are lazy or non-compliant. It is that the checklist is disconnected from the work.
The engineer writes the report in one application, then fills in a form in another
application, then saves the form in a third location. The form asks questions about
the report, but it does not interact with the report. It cannot verify that the
standard referenced in Section 3.2 is current. It cannot check that the borehole
numbers in the data tables match the borehole numbers in the interpretive text. It
cannot flag that the template being used is from two revisions ago.

A checklist that requires a human to verify things that a machine could verify is not
quality assurance. It is administrative theatre.

## The disciplines that solved this

`[GRAEME: verify]` Not every engineering discipline has this problem to the same degree.
Environmental engineering and water engineering reports, in many firms, are produced
from standardised templates with structured sections, consistent formatting, and
built-in checks. The template enforces a minimum structure. The reviewer's job is to
assess the engineering content, not to hunt for formatting errors and missing sections.

Geotechnical engineering, by contrast, tends to produce reports by copying a previous
project's report and modifying it. The "template" is whatever the last similar job
looked like. This means every report starts from a slightly different baseline, with
different formatting, different section structures, and different inherited errors.

`[GRAEME: verify]` The difference is not about the quality of the engineers. It is
about whether the discipline has invested in standardised templates that remove an
entire category of errors from the reviewer's plate. Where templates exist and are
enforced, reviewers spend their time on engineering judgement. Where templates do not
exist, reviewers spend their time on formatting, consistency, and structure -- work
that should not require a senior engineer's attention.

## What actually works: embedding checks in the workflow

The manufacturing and software industries solved this problem decades ago. The
principle is called poka-yoke (mistake-proofing): do not rely on inspection to catch
errors. Design the process so that errors cannot be introduced in the first place, or
are caught immediately when they occur.

`[GRAEME: verify]` Applied to geotechnical report production, this means: do not ask a
reviewer to verify that the correct standard is referenced -- make the standard
reference automatic. Do not ask a reviewer to check that all required sections are
present -- enforce the section structure through the template. Do not ask a reviewer
to spot copy-paste errors from the previous project -- flag project-specific content
that has not been updated.

This is not futuristic. It is the direction that every serious engineering firm's
digital strategy points towards. The question is not whether embedded QA replaces
checklist QA. The question is how long firms continue to burn senior engineers' time
on errors that should never reach them.

## Where this leaves us

The geotechnical industry has a QA problem that is not about competence. The engineers
are good. The reviewers are diligent. The problem is structural: the QA process was
designed for paper, adapted poorly for digital, and now depends on a small number of
senior people catching everything.

`[GRAEME: verify]` The firms that solve this will not solve it by adding more checklists.
They will solve it by embedding quality checks into the report production workflow
itself -- so that errors are caught at the point of creation, not at the point of
final review.

The review form nobody signs is not a compliance failure. It is a signal that the
process was never fit for purpose. The answer is not a better form. The answer is
removing the need for the form entirely.
