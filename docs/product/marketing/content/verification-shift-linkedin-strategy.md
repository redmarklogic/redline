## Diagnosis

Redline is pre-launch, pre-revenue, with a single founder. The binding constraints are:
(a) zero brand awareness in the target market, (b) zero marketing budget, and
(c) founder time is the only resource. The theoretical constraint is "scaling content
production" -- irrelevant until there is an audience to scale to.

LinkedIn organic content is the only channel that fits all three binding constraints.
It costs nothing, it reaches the target buyer (Technical Directors and Principals at
NZ/AU geotechnical consultancies), and it can be done in 30-60 minutes per day.

The founder holds a Blue Ocean position: credibility in both geotechnical engineering
and AI-assisted software workflows. Almost no one in the civil engineering space has
both. This is the positioning to exploit.

## Core Positioning

The founder stands at the intersection of geotechnical engineering and AI-assisted
workflows. The content strategy uses this to position the founder as a translator --
not a prophet -- who converts documented lessons from software engineering into
actionable guidance for civil engineers.

The core narrative arc: "Software developers hit this problem 18 months ago. Here is
what they learned. Here is what it means for you."

This arc works because:

1. It is not speculative ("AI might do X"). It is documented ("AI did X to programmers,
   here is the data").
2. It positions the founder as translator, not vendor. Trust before transaction.
3. It provides infinite content. The software world publishes continuously (DORA
   reports, industry surveys, Stack Overflow developer surveys). Every new finding is
   a new post.

## Five Content Themes

### Theme 1: The Verification Shift

The bottleneck moved from writing to reviewing. Senior engineers are now the scarce
resource, not writing capacity. Investing in AI writing tools without investing in
review infrastructure is like buying a faster printer without hiring editors.

**Source**: DORA 2024 on AI-generated code requiring higher cognitive load to review.
Cross-mapped to GE magazine evidence of report review burden.

### Theme 2: AI Amplifies Your Culture

Poor QA culture plus AI equals bad reports faster. Strong QA culture (checklists,
review gates, templates) plus AI equals amplified quality. The platform determines the
AI outcome, not the AI tool.

**Source**: Software internal developer platform research. Parallel to engineering
firms with structured QA vs. firms relying on trust-based review.

### Theme 3: The Junior Engineer Training Gap

AI can write the outline report, but who trains the future Project Directors? The
productive struggle that builds expertise is being bypassed. Firms cutting junior roles
for AI efficiency are eating their seed corn.

**Industry voices already raising this**: Jim De Waele (BGA) and Domenico Lombardi
(A-squared) have spoken publicly about the training gap. Quoting them (with
attribution) lends authority and shows the founder is connected to the conversation,
not starting it from scratch.

**Source**: Software "expertise paradox" research. Jim De Waele's BGA remarks.

### Theme 4: Batch Size Discipline

AI makes it easy to generate 40-page reports. It does not make it safe. Every
additional page is additional review burden on the scarcest person in the firm.
Small, focused deliverables beat AI-generated comprehensive documents.

**Source**: Software PR size research (500-line rule). Cross-mapped to report review
workload.

### Theme 5: Measure Quality, Not Quantity

Reports issued per month is a vanity metric. Client query rate is a sanity metric.
Are AI-generated reports being queried more by clients? More Requests for Information?
Throughput without stability is rework in disguise.

**Source**: DORA four key metrics framework translated to engineering deliverables.

## LinkedIn Comment Templates

These templates are for responding to other people's posts. They establish presence
without requiring original content. Each template seeds the "verification shift" frame
into an existing conversation.

**For posts about using AI to write reports:**

> "Software developers saw this same productivity gain 18 months ago. What they found
> next: review time per PR increased 2-3x because reviewers couldn't use familiarity
> with the author's thinking to speed-read. The bottleneck didn't disappear -- it moved
> from writing to reviewing. Curious if you're seeing the same shift with TR/PD review
> time?"

**For posts about AI tools (Copilot, ChatGPT):**

> "The DORA research on 1,110 engineers found that AI-generated artifacts require
> higher cognitive load to review per line than human-written ones -- because the
> reviewer can't trace the author's reasoning process. In geotechnical reports, where
> interpretive judgment is the value, this seems like it would hit even harder. What's
> your experience?"

**For posts about junior engineer development:**

> "Jim De Waele raised this at BGA: 'Who will be training the experienced engineers of
> the future?' Software teams call this the 'expertise paradox' -- AI bypasses the
> productive struggle that builds judgment. The engineers who can evaluate AI output are
> built through years of doing it manually first."

**For posts about AI and efficiency:**

> "Efficiency at what? Software teams learned that AI inflated throughput while
> degrading stability -- more PRs merged, more bugs shipped. The metric that matters
> isn't reports produced. It's reports that survive without queries, change orders, or
> reinterpretation. Are we measuring the right thing?"

### Surviving the Round Test

Short runway (3-6 months): LinkedIn comments and bi-weekly posts cost only founder
time. This is the minimum viable thought leadership investment -- zero spend, 30-60
minutes per day. Justified even on the shortest runway because brand awareness is the
binding constraint for Bet 1 (free wedge) user acquisition.

Long runway (2+ years): Monthly articles and speaking engagements are justified only
if the short-runway activities demonstrate engagement (see success metrics below). Do
not invest in articles or speaking pitches until the comment and post cadence has run
for at least 8 weeks and produced measurable engagement.
