## Background & Constraints

### Why we need a new name
- **Redline Logic** is occupied — a US defense firm ("rapid, integrated capability development for defense and national security"). Different sector, but same name, a LinkedIn page, and a US trademark risk. Founder does not want SEO collision or name confusion.
- **redlinelogic.com** is taken. Fallback would be `redlinelogic.io` — acceptable only if no better name exists.
- **Faultless** — off-limits (copyright concern from former employer).
- **Redline** alone (working name) — the founder is *not attached* to "Redline." The word was chosen because "redlining" is the industry term for marking up engineering documents. That etymology can be retained, or dropped entirely.

### Binding naming constraints
1. No obvious trademark conflicts in NZ and AU (IPANZ + IP Australia search required before finalising)
2. Domain available: `.com` preferred; `.co.nz` / `.nz` acceptable for NZ beachhead; `.io` only if target audience is developer-adjacent (it is not)
3. No AI signal in the name — explicit founder rule
4. Not already held by a competing or adjacent company in geotechnical, engineering QA, or document review
5. Registerable as a company in NZ; preferably also in AU and US
6. **Must not be geotechnical-specific** — the product will expand to other disciplines in civil engineering and environmental science that share the same problem: issuing documents and requiring structured review. A name that signals geotechnical-only (e.g., "geo-" prefix, "CPT", "sonde", "soil") will become a misnomer as soon as the second discipline is onboarded. The name must be discipline-agnostic at inception.

### Product context (for evaluating fit)
- **What it does**: Quality-layer infrastructure for engineering document review — starting with geotechnical, expanding to civil engineering and environmental science disciplines that share the same pattern: issuing documents (reports, assessments, baseline studies) and requiring structured, standards-based review.
- **Who uses it**: Junior–intermediate engineers (pre-review), senior/principal engineers (review efficiency), firm principals (quality governance) — across *any* regulated technical discipline, not only geotechnical.
- **Core UX**: Document goes in; quality issues come out. "Redlining" (mark-up) is the existing practitioner metaphor.
- **Framing**: Infrastructure, not a tool. Switzerland-neutral. Not AI. Not a chatbot.
- **Beachhead**: NZ + AU geotechnical consultancies
- **Expansion disciplines**: Water / hydraulic engineering, environmental science, structural engineering, geoenvironmental — any field with a regulated document-issuance-and-review workflow
- **GTM motion**: Product-led growth — product sells itself via free tools and organic discovery

### Practitioner Connotation — Graeme's Assessment

Graeme evaluated "Redmark" (without "Logic") on first-word association, "red" component, "mark" component, cross-discipline test, formal document header, and PI culture.

**Verdict from Graeme: Positive connotation overall.**

Key findings:
- **"Red" in engineering**: dominant association is red-pen mark-up on drawings, redlining. This is physical muscle memory for practitioners. "Red = failure" reads only in RAG/dashboard contexts — it does not dominate when the word is attached to a document review product.
- **"Mark" component**: mark-up, benchmark, hallmark — all professional, neutral-to-positive. Mild "schoolteacher with red pen" cultural association exists but does not survive a professional peer context.
- **Combined "Redmark"**: The portmanteau of "redline" + "markup" is traceable without explanation. The join is clean. It does not feel contrived.
- **Cross-discipline test (P13)**: Mark-up vocabulary is universal across geotechnical, water, structural, and environmental engineering. No geotechnical-specific connotation. **P13 satisfied.**
- **Formal document header**: "Prepared with Redmark quality framework" — reads as credible and professional. Passing result.
- **PI culture**: "Redmark" implies an annotation tool operated by the engineer. "Marking up" is explicitly a human act. Does not imply the tool makes professional determinations. **PI-safe.**

> *"I would take this name seriously. As a principal geotech reviewing a competitor's product, if I saw 'Redmark quality framework' in an appendix, I would not dismiss it. I would read the next line to understand what it does — and that is the correct response to a name: curiosity, not rejection."*
>
> *"The practical blocker remains the domain. redmark.com is taken. That is an operational constraint, not a connotation problem."*

### Strategic Assessment — Ron

Ron flagged one critical gate before any evaluation: **WHOIS check on `redmark.com` first** — now confirmed taken (Colorado, active since 1998). This resolves the foundational premise of the friend's argument.

Against the 13 naming principles: "Redmark" satisfies most principles. The primary concern is P6 (the "Burton@" email-domain rule) — `burton@redmark.co.nz` is functional but signals a regional NZ firm rather than a platform. The `.com` path is blocked.

On anchoring: "Redmark" is still the "Red-" prefix with "line" replaced by "mark." The question remains whether the founder is choosing this name on its own merits or because they have not let go of "Redline." A genuine clean-break test: would the founder be equally attracted to "Assura" or "Benchline" if they had never called the product "Redline"?

## "Redmark Logic" — Candidate Evaluation (2026-06-01)

The founder proposed "Redmark Logic" as a new candidate — available as `.com` and on the NZ company register. Ron, John, and Graeme evaluated it using the following mental models: **Reversible vs Irreversible**, **Sunk-Cost Fallacy**, **Cognitive Biases (Anchoring)**, **Pre-mortem**, **Inversion**, and **Trade-off Analysis**.

### Mental Model Analysis

**Reversible vs Irreversible (One-Way Door)**
Company naming is a one-way door. NZ deregistration is cheap; the lock-in is not legal — it is reputational and operational. Corporate filings, trademark window, email addresses, codebase references, and customer memory all compound with every week the name is in production. The bar for proceeding is high, not moderate.

**Sunk-Cost Fallacy — Anchoring on "Redline Logic"**
Both halves of "Redmark Logic" are residue from the original name:
- **"Red"** — carried forward from "Redline." No new strategic reasoning.
- **"mark"** — substituted for "line" as a minimal patch to escape the collision.
- **"Logic"** — kept verbatim from "Redline Logic," the name being abandoned.

This is a double sunk-cost anchor. The founder has not produced a new name; they have patched the old one twice. The decisive test: *Would you choose "Redmark Logic" if you had never heard of "Redline Logic"?* If the answer is honest, it is no.

**Cognitive Biases — Anchoring**
The "Red-" prefix carries zero forward brand value for a cold prospect. It exists because the founder has used "Redline" for two years. A water engineer in Christchurch encountering it for the first time will have no recognition of relevance from "Red-". Familiarity is not a naming criterion.

**Pre-mortem (18 months post-launch, the name was wrong)**
All three agents independently identified the same failure modes:
1. Engineers consistently dropped "Logic" in verbal conversation, calling it "Redmark." Two names in the market, inconsistent brand recall.
2. Prospects Googling "Redmark Logic" surfaced "Redline Logic" (the US defense firm) on page one. Google's prefix/phoneme autocomplete treats them as related queries. At least one qualified lead did not follow up, assuming the referrer had the name wrong.
3. LogiCamms — a known AU/NZ engineering services brand — generated recurring misidentification in AU. Three leads emailed the wrong company.
4. Every demo call began with two minutes clarifying "we're not IT consultants, we're a document review platform." Category confusion persisted into Year 2.
5. A US investor at Series A flagged trademark proximity to "Redline Logic" during due diligence. Material friction.
6. Two senior engineers at a Christchurch consultancy declined to champion the product internally: *"It sounds like it makes the decisions."* The word "Logic" activated PI (professional indemnity) anxiety.

**Inversion (how to guarantee the name fails)**
"Redmark Logic" systematically hits the following failure modes:
- Keep "Red-" prefix → search engines and memory conflate it with "Redline Logic" (defense)
- Add "Logic" → IT consulting register, not engineering quality infrastructure
- 15-character compound domain → hard to spell, say, and remember verbally
- "Redmark" alone → conflicts with Colorado fleet-fuel LLC at redmark.com
- Zero semantic anchor to quality, certification, verification, or review
- Sounds like "Remark Logic" when spoken quickly in a conference room
- No expansion signal for civil, structural, or environmental markets

**Trade-off Analysis — What Does "Logic" Add vs Cost?**

| Dimension | "Logic" adds | "Logic" costs |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|
| Semantic | Marginal "rules-based" signal | IT consulting register; wrong category for engineering quality layer |
| Domain | Forces use of full "redmarklogic.com" (15 chars) | Burton@redmarklogic.com is unwieldy in verbal referrals and cold email |
| Recall | None for practitioners | Collides with CoreLogic (dominant AU/NZ property data firm) in recall cluster |
| PLG surfaces | None | URL length friction, verbal misheard as "Redline Logic", poor LinkedIn signal |
| PI culture | None | "Logic" implies the tool makes decisions — activates professional indemnity anxiety |

Net verdict on "Logic": negative. It costs more than it adds on every dimension.

### Practitioner Reaction (Graeme)

> "Redmark" lands well. Every engineer knows what a red pen means on a drawing. "Logic" immediately signals software company. My first thought is decision-support automation — possibly AI-driven. Not quality infrastructure.
>
> "Logic" carries specific freight for engineers: Boolean rules, automated if/then decisions, enterprise software (Oracle NetSuite, Rockwell Logix, defense primes). It does NOT signal rigour or precision the way "standard," "review," "check," or "benchmark" would.
>
> "Redmark Logic" and "Redline Logic" are one phoneme apart. In spoken NZ/AU English at speed, they are indistinguishable. The collision risk is not theoretical — it is near-certain in verbal conversation.
>
> My strongest objection is PI culture. Engineers are personally and professionally liable for their judgements. A tool called "Logic" implies the tool exercises judgement. That is the exact signal the product must NOT send. "Logic" will activate PI anxiety in exactly the senior practitioners who need to champion adoption internally.
>
> **Drop "Logic." "Redmark" is cleaner, stronger, and more professionally credible. The only reason "Logic" is on the table is because it was in the name you had to abandon. That is not a good enough reason to keep it.**

### Additional Disqualification: "Proofmark" (P7 flag)

Ron and John separately noted that **Proofmark** (on the agreed shortlist) carries a secondary risk not previously flagged: "proof-house" or "proof-mark" is the term for the mark stamped on firearms after ballistics testing. In the context of a B2B infrastructure tool, this is a mild P7 (no defense/surveillance/adversarial connotations) flag. Low severity but worth noting for NZ/AU markets. Not a hard disqualifier — context of use makes it clear — but the founder should be aware.

### Verdict: Reject "Redmark Logic" (Unanimous)

| **John** | Reject | Wrong B2B register ("Logic" = IT consulting), phonetic collision with defense firm persists, PLG surfaces broken |
