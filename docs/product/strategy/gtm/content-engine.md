# Content Engine — Founder LinkedIn Cadence and Dream 100

**Status**: Draft v2. **Owner**: Founder (executes); Ron (custodian).
**Anchored to**: `gtm/2026-launch-plan.md` (Channel Plan), `non-goals.md` (no paid
acquisition H1, no scaled AI content), Bet 4 (Switzerland-neutral).

> This document specifies the *how* of the founder's LinkedIn channel — the cadence,
> the content rubric, and the targeted-outreach mechanic. The launch plan committed to
> founder-led LinkedIn but did not say what to post or how often. Solo founders who do
> not pre-commit a sustainable rhythm post sporadically, burn out, and conclude the
> channel "doesn't work".

## Hard Constraints (From Strategy)

0. **Pre-launch period (Apr 18 – May 31).** Founder is employed. LinkedIn content
   preparation (drafting, batching, research) is permitted on personal time. Posting
   may begin before June 1 — LinkedIn is personal, not employer IP. Product
   announcements and "launch" posts wait until the product is live (post-June 1).
1. **No paid spend in H1** (`non-goals.md` GTM #3).
2. **No scaled AI-generated content** — content quality is a Switzerland-neutral
   trust signal; degraded EEAT (Experience, Expertise, Authority, Trust) signals
   forfeit positioning. See `non-goals.md`.
3. **Founder-only voice in H1** — Mark and Ron do not post under their names; the
   channel is the founder's. Ghostwriting assistance from Mark is allowed; final
   approval is the founder's.
4. **Geographic discipline** — content addresses NZ + AU geotech only; no global
   posturing (Bet 5).

## Cadence

| Activity | Frequency | Time/day |
|---|---|---|
| Original post | 2–3 per week (Mon–Thu best) | (drafting batched on Sunday) |
| Thoughtful comments on others' posts | 10 per 1 own post (the 10:1 rule) | 15 min morning |
| DM replies, target-account engagement, connection requests | Daily | 15–30 min afternoon |
| Total founder time on channel | — | 30–60 min/day |

The 10:1 commenting rule is the single highest-leverage habit. Comments build the
algorithm signal that boosts the founder's own posts and create the warm introductions
that Phase-2 outreach needs.

## Content Rubric — The Big 5

Adapted from Marcus Sheridan's *They Ask, You Answer*. The five topic categories
proven to drive B2B buyer trust. Every founder post must map to one of these.

| Big 5 category | What to post | Geotech-specific examples |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Pricing & Costs** | Honest framing of cost questions buyers won't ask vendors directly | "What does a senior-engineer review round actually cost a 10-person firm?" "When does an in-house QA tool pay back?" *(NB: do not publish Redline pricing in H1; pricing is parked.)* |
| **2. Problems** | What goes wrong, the failure modes, the things vendors hide | "Five ways AI writing tools quietly damage a GBR." "Why ChatGPT can't help with NZGS-grounded interpretive content." |
| **3. Versus & Comparisons** | Honest comparisons with alternatives | "Senior peer review vs. tooling: where each one wins." "Redline (when named) vs. ChatGPT vs. Microsoft Copilot — which job each one is actually for." |
| **4. Reviews** | Customer voices, case fragments, anonymised stories | "What an intermediate engineer told me about her last review round." (Use anonymised KR2 conversation insights — with permission.) |
| **5. Best in Class** | Curated rankings, "best" lists, recognised standards | "The five NZS / AS standards every junior geotech engineer should re-read this year." |

The **Problems** and **Versus** categories are the highest-converting in B2B —
prioritise them. They directly address the buyer's silent question: "what could go
wrong if I pick the wrong tool?"

## Voice Constraints (Binding)

The founder is **not** a geotechnical engineer (see `positioning.md` →
Credibility Boundaries). Big 5 content must therefore be written from one of three
permitted positions:

1. **Citing the literature.** "NZGS guidance on [X] expects...", "CIRIA C760 sets out...",
   "Eurocode 7 clause [Y] requires...". Anchor every interpretive claim to a named,
   citeable source from the curated corpus.
2. **Citing publicly available cases.** NZ court judgements on Differing Site
   Conditions, Ground Engineering magazine post-mortems, published failure case studies.
   Public material only — never anonymised internal anecdote dressed up as a public case.
3. **Speaking as the data scientist who watched the process.** "For 3.5 years I sat
   inside a geotech team as their data scientist and watched [X process play out Y way]."
   This is honest because it describes observation of workflow, not engineering judgement.

**Forbidden in every Big 5 post:**

- First-person engineering claims ("in my projects...", "I've reviewed...",
  "in my experience as an engineer...").
- Inventing case fragments (the *Reviews* category uses anonymised KR2 discovery
  insights *with permission*, not fabricated composites).
- Specific design, calculation, or interpretive opinions presented as the founder's
  professional view.

**Test before publishing every post:** "Could a senior NZ geotech engineer who knows
the founder personally read this and conclude he is misrepresenting his credentials?"
If yes, rewrite or kill.

## Content Mix Within the Big 5

Within each post, follow the 4-1-1 rule (Joe Pulizzi):

- 4 of every 6 posts: curated insight from others (cited engineering authors, NZGS
  guidance updates, public case law on Differing Site Conditions claims) with the
  founder's added commentary.
- 1 of every 6 posts: original educational content (the founder's own POV).
- 1 of every 6 posts: a direct invitation (free Skeleton Generator link, "DM me if
  you'd like a 30-min discovery chat").

Three post archetypes — rotate weekly:

1. **Insight** — teach something new in 200 words. Builds authority.
2. **Point-of-view** — contrarian take or reframe. Stops the scroll.
3. **Case fragment** — anonymised KR2 story or operational anecdote. Builds trust.

## Dream 100 — Targeted Outreach Mechanic

A "Dream 100" account list is the founder's hand-picked target firms (start with 30,
not 100, given NZ/AU market size). Outreach to each follows a four-phase mechanic
(Blount / Tillman, *The AI Edge*; cadence overlaid on Carbary's *Content-Based
Networking* mechanic):

### Phase 1 — Familiarity (LCS: Like, Comment, Share) — 2–3 weeks

Like and leave a *thoughtful* comment on the prospect's posts. Not "great post" —
substantive engagement that demonstrates the founder read it. This crosses the
prospect's familiarity threshold before any DM lands.

### Phase 2 — Personalised connection request

Reference the specific post or shared context. **Do not pitch in the connection
request** — that triggers the bait-and-switch reflex and burns the relationship.

### Phase 3 — Indirect touch (give before asking)

After connection, send one valuable resource via DM with no ask. Examples:

- A relevant clause from a recent NZS update they may not have seen.
- An anonymised insight from a recent KR2 conversation.
- A short article the founder has written.

### Phase 4 — Direct ask (the 4-step DM)

Only after Phases 1–3 have warmed the relationship:

- **Hook**: subject line tailored to their situation.
- **Relate**: demonstrate empathy with the specific pain.
- **Value-bridge**: connect their problem to how a 30-minute chat could help them.
- **Ask**: clear, low-pressure CTA — "Would you have 30 minutes for a quick chat?
  I'm trying to understand how firms like yours handle [X]. No pitch."

Note: Phase 4 maps directly to the KR2 discovery interview opener — see
`discovery-guide.md`.

## Content-Based Networking Hack (Carbary)

For the highest-value Dream 100 prospects (Principals, Partners) who are unlikely to
respond to a cold DM:

> Invite them to be interviewed for a piece of content (a podcast episode, a written
> Q&A, a roundup post titled "How three NZ Principals approach GBR review")
> instead of pitching them.

People rarely turn down an opportunity to be quoted as an expert. The interview
becomes the relationship. The content becomes the asset.

This is the founder's only viable path to Phase-2 buyer (Principal/Partner)
relationships in H1, given the explicit non-goal of enterprise sales motion.

## Product-Led SEO — Deferred Decision

Eli Schwartz's Product-Led SEO methodology (from the digital-marketing notebook)
describes building a *product* — typically a tool, database, or programmatic page
template — that captures organic search demand at scale, rather than writing blog
posts to chase keywords.

The Sprint-1 GBR Skeleton Generator is structurally compatible with this pattern:
each generated skeleton could become a public, indexed page (with customer
permission), and template-driven landing pages keyed to specific NZS standards
could capture "NZS 3910 GBR template" search demand.

**This is not committed in H1.** Reasons:

- Product-Led SEO requires patience; pages take months to rank. KR1 is a 60-day
  metric. The two timelines don't align.
- Privacy: customer documents must not become public artifacts without
  explicit per-document consent. The zero-training perimeter (engineering non-goal
  #1) bleeds into a zero-publication default.
- Engineering load: programmatic page generation is real architecture work that
  competes with the Sprint 2–3 Pre-Review build.

Decision parked under `decisions/parked-decisions.md` P-019. Revisit at H2 strategy
refresh once the Skeleton Generator has shipped and consent UX is designed.

## Generative AI in Content — Hard Rule

**No mass-produced AI-generated SEO content.** This is a non-goal (`non-goals.md`).

The founder's hypothesis — that an LLM acting as a "Judge" can quality-gate
AI-assisted content — is *validated* by current content-marketing literature.
Practitioners specifically endorse using one LLM (e.g., Claude) to review another
LLM's draft (e.g., ChatGPT) before a human reviewer signs off. So:

- AI assistance for *drafting* a founder post: allowed, with founder rewrite.
- LLM-as-Judge quality gate before posting: required.
- Mass programmatic AI-generated blog spam: forbidden. Google's "scaled content
  abuse" classifier penalises sites that scale from a few articles per week to
  hundreds; case studies show 99%+ ranking loss. The Switzerland-neutral
  positioning cannot survive that reputation hit.

## Measurement

The channel is measured against KR1 (signups) and KR2 (qualified conversations) —
not against vanity metrics like impressions or follower count.

| Channel-level metric | Why it matters | Target |
|---|---|---|
| Signups attributable to LinkedIn | Direct contribution to KR1 | ≥ 25 of the KR1 50 (50% mix) |
| Discovery conversations sourced from LinkedIn | Direct contribution to KR2 | ≥ 8 of the KR2 15 (>50% mix) |
| Followers, impressions, "engagement rate" | Vanity unless they convert (Lean Startup test — see OKRs) | Tracked, not targeted |
