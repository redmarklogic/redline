## Workplace Safety Law Changes
The  Health  and  Safety  at  Work  Amendment  Bill
(January  2026)  proposes  amendments to the  Health
and  Safety  at  Work  Act  2015  (the  HSW  Act),  the
WorkSafe New Zealand Act 2013 (the WorkSafe Act),
and the Health and Safety at Work (General Risk and
## Workplace Management) Regulations 2016.
The stated objectives of the Bill are to:
reduce unnecessary compliance costs;
increase      certainty      for      businesses      and
organisations about what they need to do; and
support continued reductions in the incidence of
workplace fatalities, injuries, and illnesses.
The  Bill  seeks  to  re focus  the  workplace  health  and
safety  system  on  critical  risks,  clarifying  areas  of
confusion,    creating    greater    certainty    through
strengthening  approved  codes  of  practice  (ACOPs),
and prioritising regulators’ functions.
The Bill adds a new definition of small PCBUs (person
conducting a business or undertaking) with the basic
requirement that there are fewer than 20 workers.
In  simple  terms,  the  government’s  goal  is  to  reduce
the “red tape” for smaller businesses. Unfortunately,
some  big  businesses  are  already  signalling  that  they
will introduce so-called “blue tape” conditions in their
contracts  to  require  small  businesses  to  carry  on
managing non-critical health and safety issues,  even if
the   law   changes.   These   larger   organisations   are
believed  to  include  Woolworths,  Downer  NZ,  and
Ports of Auckland.
It  is  reasonable  to  expect  that  many  other  clients
would  introduce  contractual  obligations  to  ensure
that  there  was  only  one  set  of  health  and  safety
standards in operation on their work sites.
Whilst the present health and safety legislation has its
critics, at least the requirements are common across
the  construction  industry.  The  push  to  help  small
businesses by allowing them to focus on critical risks
could  result  in  more  complex  conditions  of  contract
with clients –   something that could step further away
from   the   common   law   conditions   we   presently
advocate for.
The  impact  of  the changes  proposed  as  part  of  the
Health and Safety at Work Amendment Bill is already
flowing     into     other     Worksafe     documentation
presently under review. The legislation would change
the  legal  status  of  ACOPs  to  provide  a  type  of  legal
protection referred to as a “safe harbour”. This means
that  if  a  business  follows  an  ACOP,  they  would  be
treated  as  having  complied  with  the  relevant  legal
duty. WorkSafe is currently developing a   draft code of
practice  for  roles  and  responsibilities  in  residential
construction,  which envisages the legislation change.
As   of   31 March   2026,   the   Bill   is   before   select
committee.
Which Insurance Policies should
you Carry?
With renewals a couple of months away, it is a good
time  to  review  your  insurance  cover.  What  are  the
insurance policies that a typical consulting firm should
carry and broadly what do they cover?
## Professional Indemnity
The main insurance policy is Professional Indemnity –
covering the insured for legal liability to third parties
connected to providing or not providing professional
services.
## Companion Insurance
Companion insurance refers  to other types of liability
insurance that cover areas of risk not included in the
professional indemnity policy.
Their  order  of  importance  will  depend  on  your  own
circumstances and risk appetite.
## Public Liability / General Liability
Generally,   second   to   professional   indemnity   (PI)
insurance is Public Liability, sometimes referred to as
General Liability insurance.
Often  required  in  contract  terms,  including  lease
agreements,  this  policy  covers  your  legal  liability  for
property damage or personal injury to third parties.
## Statutory Liability
This is the next most commonly held insurance policy
and  covers  action  by  a  regulatory  body  for  certain
breaches of New Zealand statute and court-imposed
fines or reparation orders.
WorkSafe New Zealand can investigate and prosecute
a business, primarily under the Health and Safety at
Work  Act  2015  and  Ministry  for  the  Environment
enforces the Resource Management Act.
Not  all  fines  are  insurable  (certain  Acts  prohibit
insurance  cover),   but  most  reparations  are,  as  are
associated defence costs.
## Cyber Liability
Cyber  loss  is  becoming  an  increasingly  frequent  risk
exposure  and  most  business  should  be  considering
Cyber  Liability  as  part  of  their  risk  management
programme.
The insurance provides cover for the businesses’  own
losses, ‘first party’, due to cyber-related activity such
as  ransomware  or    data  encryption;   and liability  to
third   parties   following   a   cyber   event   through
corruption of the business systems.
Other  companion  policies  relate  to  management
liability  and  are  more  relevant  to  limited  liability
companies  with  multiple  directors  and  or  business
with employees.
## Directors & Officers Liability
This  insurance provides  protection  to  directors  and
company officers for personal liability they may have
for the decisions they or fellow directors make in the
management of the company.

Sometimes  called  Employment Disputes Insurance,

by    employees    (including    previous    or    future
employees)    and   relates to   compensation   claims
relating      to      unfair      dismissal,      discrimination,
harassment,   and   certain   other   breaches   of   the
## Employment Relations Act.
## Employers Liability
This insurance also covers action against the business
or  employers  by  employees  but  relates  to  personal
injuries not  covered  under  ACC.  This  is  at  the  lower
end of the risk scale,  given that our ACC indemnity will
compensate  most  work-related  injuries. Some  types
of   compensation   claims   could   include where   an
employee suffers a gradual injury over a long period
of  time,  or  the  injury  relates  to  mental  anguish  or
stress.
## Legal Defence Costs Inclusion
All the above policies include the legal defence costs
associated  with  managing  claims.  Having  this  cover
provides  access  to  qualified  lawyers  and  experts  to
place  you  in  a  good  defensible  position  and  reduce
the  potential  for  reputational  damage  and  wasted
time in dealing with these situations.

## Lawyers
https://www.theguardian.com/australia-

A  lawyer  has  been  referred  to  Western  Australia’s
legal  regulator  after  using  artificial  intelligence  to
prepare    court  documents  for  an  immigration  case.
The documents contained AI-generated case citations
for cases that did not exist.
It  is  one  of  more  than  20  cases  so  far  in  Australia  in
which  AI  use  has  resulted  in  fake  citations  or  other
errors   in   court   submissions,   with   warnings   from
judges  across  the  country  to  be  wary  of  using  the
technology in the legal profession.
In a federal court judgment published this week, the
anonymised lawyer was referred to the Legal Practice
Board  of  Western  Australia  for  consideration  and
ordered  to  pay  the  federal  government’s  costs  of
$8,371.30  after  submissions  to  an  immigration  case
were found by the representative for the immigration
minister  to  include  four  case  citations  that  did  not
exist.
Justice Arran Gerrard said the incident “demonstrates
the  inherent  dangers  associated  with  practitioners
solely relying on the use of artificial intelligence in the
preparation of court documents and the way in which
that interacts with a practitioner’s duty to the court”.
## The $60,000 Question*
Since January this year the Disputes Tribunal can now
consider  claims  up  to  $60,000.  That’s  double  the
previous limit of $30,000. This makes more likely:
Claimants making more use of the Disputes Tribunal
generally,  as  an  economical  way  to  recover  their
losses (or perceived losses).
Risks of “blowback” from seeking unpaid fees –   your
claim might be $5,000 in fees but the counter-claim
could be ugly!
The  Disputes  Tribunal  has  some  special  attributes
including  that  you  can’t  be  represented  by  a  lawyer
and   that   decisions   are   based   on   “fairness”   not
contract –    in  some  situations  the  Disputes  Tribunal
can  even  unpick  a  “full  and  final”  settlement  that  is
considered   by   the   Tribunal   to   be   unfair.   These
attributes are not necessarily helpful to engineers so,
notify early and seek advice!

## Historical Case Study
The following case study was first published to CEAS
members in June 1979.
These  case  histories  are  intended  to  be  for  the
guidance and information of members in their efforts
to  avoid  claims.  However,  members  are  warned  of
the danger of attempting to deal with a possible claim
situation themselves by applying these case histories
to their own case where a possible claim has arisen or
may arise. In all situations where there is a likelihood
of  a  claim  being  made,  the  appropriate  assistance
must be sought at an early stage.
## Facts
An Engineer was asked to report on the condition of a
site for a building by an Architect. The Engineer was
advised by the Architect that the investigation should
be  limited  as  to  cost.  The  Engineer  agreed  to  place
three  hand  auger  holes  and  reported  favourably  on
the site.
Subsequently, when the building work began, it was
found that there was a considerable amount of loose
filling on the site at a position away from where the
test had been made.
The  owner  sued  the  Engineer  for  negligence  on  the
grounds  that  they  had  been  put  to  unexpected
expense  after  relying  on  the  Engineer's  appraisal  of
the site.
## Professional Liability Lesson
The  Engineer  should  have  fully  qualified  their  report
by  pointing  out  the  limits  of  the  investigation  and
saying that the report’s conclusion was based on this
information only.
It is very important that where any restriction on costs
is imposed by the client that the Engineer should draw
attention   to   this   in   their   report   and   qualify   it
accordingly.
