# Practice of Engineering vs. Business of Engineering

**Date**: 2026-05-03
**Research question**: Should we position the Redline quality layer squarely in the "business of engineering" lane? Does this framing resonate with CEOs? Where does it leave calculation-checking as a future feature?
**Actor**: Ron (Strategy & GTM Advisor)
**Triggered by**: Founder Brain Dump Agenda item 2.2

---

## Summary

The founder draws an explicit distinction between two layers of value in
geotechnical engineering: the **practice of engineering** (physical equations,
engineering theory, calculation checks) and the **business of engineering**
(standards compliance for insurance, liability, and legal protection). A design
can work physically but still expose the firm to liability if it doesn't comply
with standards.

Five knowledge bases were queried. The evidence converges on a clear strategic
recommendation: position squarely in the business-of-engineering lane, with
calculation-checking held back as a Phase 2 expansion.

---

## Evidence from Knowledge Bases

### Founder Memos

The founder explicitly names this distinction and categorises Redline on the
business side:

> "There is engineering as a science or practice and then there is the business
> of engineering... if we are checking the calculation of a retaining wall, even
> if we are checking if a certain design flies with engineering standards, I
> think these are actually two different cases. This [calculation] is the
> practice of engineering. There is theory — physical equations plus engineering
> judgment. And checking against standards, engineering standards and guidelines
> [is the business of engineering]." — Founder Memo [1]

> "You might [have] something that works really well. But if it doesn't comply
> with standards, then you are not insured and you're exposing yourself to
> liability and you know maybe even criminal charges." — Founder Memo [2]

The founder does not propose building calculation-checking tools. All product
features are focused on standards-checking and document compliance: the quality
layer acts as an automated reviewer to ensure engineering reports adhere to
standards before senior sign-off [6].

### Professional Services Firm Management

Leaders of small engineering consultancies view standards compliance primarily as
a **business and liability management concern**, not merely a technical
discipline issue:

- Negligence claims are frequently filed not because of actual design errors, but
  as retaliatory counterclaims when the engineering firm sues a client for
  nonpayment. A disgruntled client hires expert witnesses to testify that the firm
  performed "below the standard of care" [7].
- QC programs that enforce standards compliance serve the dual purpose of
  preventing errors and satisfying insurer requirements [3, 4].
- Keeping up with changing standards is a mandatory and expensive business cost —
  a small structural firm can spend $1,500+ per code-change cycle on standards
  updates alone [6-PSF].
- PI insurance is "one of the biggest expenses of owning an engineering firm" [2-PSF],
  and firms that lack structured QC programs face higher premiums and reduced
  coverage.

### Risk Assessment in Engineering

The legal literature is unambiguous: "Failure to conform to standards generally
accepted in the profession is strong evidence of negligence" [3-Risk]. A
physically correct design does not protect a firm if it fails to comply with
published codes of practice — courts rely on standards-based benchmarks to
determine whether "good engineering practice has been followed" [6-Risk].

Insurers evaluate internal QC procedures when setting premium rates. Firms with
strict procedures to check design quality against standards are rewarded with
lower premiums [5-Risk].

### Entrepreneurship & Startup Strategy (Crossing the Chasm)

Pragmatist buyers (the early majority) are loath to buy until they can compare
products within an established buying category. Any dialogue about competition
must be based on **market-oriented concerns**, not product-centric technical
attributes [5-Chasm, 6-Chasm].

When positioning for CEOs: "They are focused on the strategic impact of any
purchase on the performance of the organisation... less interested in the
detail — features and functions — and more concerned with business impact,
levels of risk, return on investment, supplier credibility" [1-Chasm, 2-Chasm].

### Monetizing & Scaling Innovation

"A feature belongs to the product; a benefit belongs to the customer" [1-Monit].
Customers don't buy products — they buy the benefits those products deliver.
"Talking Features and Not Benefits" is identified as a fatal flaw in value
messaging [3-Monit]. Technical jargon overcomplicates the message and leads to
customer confusion and commercial failure [4-Monit, 5-Monit].

## Strategic Conclusions

### Q1: Should we position the quality layer squarely in the "business of engineering" lane?

**Yes. Unambiguously.**

### Q2: Does this framing resonate with CEOs?

**Yes, because it maps directly to the CEO Priority Hierarchy.**

### Q3: Where does this leave calculation-checking as a future feature?

**Explicitly deferred — a Phase 2 expansion that broadens TAM but does not
define the beachhead.**
