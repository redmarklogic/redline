## Memo Summaries

### Memo 1 — Working with AI Crew (2026-04-21)

The founder frames this as a pre-mortem (an exercise to anticipate how and why a project
might fail before it launches). The tone begins with enthusiasm about AI capabilities and
transitions into introspection and vulnerability about the possibility of business failure.

**Key points:**

- The founder has created a "virtual corporation" staffed by AI agents — John (Marketing),
  Matt (UX), Ron (Strategy) — that work simultaneously without corporate friction and
  produce highly solid business plans.
- AI agents provide crucial "pushback" that shapes strategy. Ron challenged whether clients
  would trust AI-generated text without a human "watching the watchman." The founder
  resolved this by explaining there is always a human in the loop.
- AI agents' skills are static. Pivoting requires manually updating system prompts and
  skill files, or "hiring" new agents. Generic agents sometimes lack specific industry
  context (e.g., Ron occasionally misses financial nuances of civil engineering firms).
- Core concern: existential dread of execution failure. The plan is the most solid the
  founder has ever had, so market rejection would be "devastating." High opportunity cost
  of not pursuing other jobs. Track record of overconfidence on complex projects.
- Action items: (1) Create formal sprint-cadence update schedule for agent skills/prompts,
  (2) Feed AI advisors specific engineering business data and cognitive frameworks,
  (3) Spend weeks on strategy/planning before writing code.

### Memo 2 — AI Gives More Time for Strategic Work (2026-04-22)

Reflective, philosophical tone. The founder explores how AI fundamentally alters human
labour, shifting effort from tactical execution to high-level strategy.

**Key points:**

- "AI will not replace people; people using AI will replace people." But simply typing
  prompts into ChatGPT is "scratching the surface." True disruption = building an entire
  AI crew with specific tools and skills to emulate a company.
- **Specialised AI vs. generic AI**: Redline is "next generation" AI — highly customised
  for geotechnical engineering. Engineers using specialised tools will replace those using
  generic tools. (See `docs/product/marketing/messaging/specialized-ai-thesis.md`.)
- Core thesis: corporate environments are burdened by "necessary work which is not
  strategic" (emails, meetings, RFPs). AI takes over this tactical labour, freeing humans
  for high-value strategic work.
- The founder has eliminated almost all tactical labour — no meetings, AI handles
  marketing content, infrastructure, coding, testing, documentation. He operates at
  the "architectural level."
- The 20-minute "sweet spot": AI tasks take 20-30 minutes, forcing the founder to
  switch context to strategic work during wait times.
- Primary concern: complacency. Current methods give an edge, but others will catch up
  as AI becomes widely available.

## Individual Assessments

### Ron — Strategy & GTM

**What resonates:**
- Memo 1 is the most self-aware thing the founder has written. Naming the emotional bias
  ("existential dread") means it can be referenced when decision-making distorts under
  pressure.
- The "watching the watchman" insight maps directly to Redline's Switzerland-neutral,
  human-in-the-loop positioning.
- Memo 2 correctly identifies the venture's core thesis: specialised AI for a niche domain
  beats generic AI. Aligns with the Christensen disruption frame — attacking
  nonconsumption, not competing with incumbents.

**Concerns:**
1. The AI crew narrative is becoming a strategy in itself, and it is not one. The danger is
   conflating "I have built an efficient way to work" with "I have built a viable business."
   Organic-only acquisition cannot reach 50 signups in 60 days from a pool of 200-400
   NZ intermediate engineers regardless of internal productivity.
2. The complacency concern is correctly identified but incorrectly framed. The real risk is
   not that competitors adopt AI generically — it is that a competitor with domain expertise
   AND AI tooling enters the geotechnical review space. The moat is the Standards Knowledge
   Store, not "I use AI better."
3. "Spend weeks on strategy/planning before writing code" directly contradicts Sprint 1
   timeline (June 1-30). The strategy work is done. More strategy is diminishing returns.

**Key advice:** Stop talking about the AI crew in strategy context. Confront the TAM
problem before launch. Prioritise the Standards Knowledge Store above everything else.
Do not let "more strategy" delay the build.

### Mark — Product

**What resonates:**
- The specialised-AI-beats-generic-AI thesis underpins Bet 6 (new-market disruption).
  An engineer using Redline's Standards Knowledge Store produces output ChatGPT cannot
  match. The founder is living his own product thesis.
- Ron's "watchman" challenge directly produced the Switzerland-neutral positioning and
  audit-trail decision.

**Concerns:**
1. Memo 1 reveals the founder knows the biggest risk and is not yet acting on it. All three
   action items are inward-facing — none involve talking to a customer.
2. Memo 2's "eliminated all tactical labour" claim worries because tactical labour (emails,
   meetings) generates discovery signal. If eliminated, who generates the signal that
   validates the product thesis?
3. The plan is over-validated internally and under-validated externally. Intellectual rigour
   is not market validation.

**Key advice:** Talk to five engineers before June 1. Cut Sprint 1 scope to three
deliverables. Run a Day 0 experiment (landing page with skeleton PDF). Schedule the first
Van Westendorp pricing interview for ship day.

### John — Marketing

**What resonates:**
- "People using specialised AI will replace people using generic AI" is marketing gold — a
  provocative, clear, defensible hook for 12 months of content. (See detailed thesis
  development at `docs/product/marketing/messaging/specialized-ai-thesis.md`.)
- PLG acquisition wedge (free skeleton) is a natural content magnet.
- Big 5 content white space is wide open — no competitor publishes transparent content.

**Concerns:**
1. Both memos are inward-facing. Not a single customer mentioned by name.
2. The "existential dread" signal means marketing must generate early, cheap,
   disconfirming signals. 90 days is too late; want signal at 30 days.
3. Zero social proof in a trust-dependent market. Engineers buy on peer reference.

**Key advice:** Stop building, start talking — 15 conversations become the content calendar.
Record the "AI crew" story for LinkedIn while it is fresh (founder-brand content, not
product content). Keep the brand voice boring — technical specificity and radical
transparency win trust.

### Harriet — People & Agent Development

**What resonates:**
- The founder has built a stream-aligned virtual organisation with clear file authority,
  handoff chains, and interaction modes. No status meetings, no territorial politics.
- The "pushback" dynamic works as designed — advisory agents are scoped to disagree.

**Concerns:**
1. Single point of failure is the founder, not the agents. Bus factor of one with no
   succession plan.
2. "Virtual corporation" is a dangerous metaphor. This is a one-person consultancy with
   AI-powered research assistants. None of the agents can close a deal, attend a site
   visit, or handle a customer complaint.
3. The founder conflates agent quality with team quality. Agents do not learn from each
   other, build trust over time, or adapt organically. Every learning must be manually
   encoded into a skill file.
4. Cognitive load risk: context-switching between strategic thinking, agent orchestration,
   and code architecture continuously. Invisible debt manifests as poor prioritisation.

**Key advice:** Protect the founder, not the agents. Do not confuse eliminating tactical
labour with creating strategic capacity. Plan for the first customer interaction to break
the agent model — that moment is the most valuable data point. Resist the urge to hire
more agents (cognitive-load ceiling). Monthly red-team exercise with pre-mortem.

## Board Consensus — Top 4 Recommendations

1. **Talk to 5-15 real engineers before June 1.** Show skeleton output (even manually
   generated). Ask: "Would this have saved you time?" Single highest-leverage activity.
2. **Cut Sprint 1 scope to 3 deliverables.** Skeleton generator + web surface + Standards
   Knowledge Store. Drop audit log and document parser to Sprint 2.
3. **Run a Day 0 experiment.** Landing page with skeleton sample PDF, gated behind a
   work-email form, promoted via LinkedIn. 20 emails = signal. Zero = trouble.
4. **Protect the founder.** Build a founder runbook. Use freed time for customer outreach,
   not more agent configuration. The most fragile component is the human.

## Can This Business Succeed?

**Unanimous answer: yes, but only if one critical correction is made to the current trajectory.**

The plan has never been touched by a customer. The AI crew has produced the most
intellectually rigorous pre-revenue strategy any advisor has seen. But every artefact was
produced inside a closed loop — founder inputs, agent outputs, founder refines, agents
respond. Zero external signal. The next move is outward, not inward.
