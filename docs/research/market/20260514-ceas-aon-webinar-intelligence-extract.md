## Insurance Taxonomy — CAS/AON Framework for NZ Consulting Engineers

CAS groups liability policies into four main categories. This is the mental model the
insurer uses and, by extension, the mental model that CAS member firms hold.

### 1. Professional Indemnity (PI)

**What it covers:** Pure financial loss caused by errors or omissions in professional
services — design faults, calculation errors, advice, inspections.

**Policy type:** Claims-made (policy must be active when the problem is *discovered*,
not when the work was done). Continuity of cover is critical — gaps create retroactive
date risk.

**Key features of the CAS policy (NZI as the underwriter):**
- Two automatic reinstatements of the policy limit (effectively 3× the limit per year)
- Cost-exclusive deductible: policyholder pays nothing if the claim is successfully
  defended (most PI policies require the deductible regardless of outcome)
- Cover for legal costs of attending court or disciplinary investigations
- Global coverage except USA/Canada (optional extension available)
- Exclusions: known prior claims, deliberate/fraudulent acts, contractual guarantees
  of outcome, certain statutory fines (Health and Safety at Work Act)

**Typical claim scenarios:**
- Design error that only becomes apparent after a structure is built
- Calculation error leading to premature failure of an asset

**QA dimension (directly relevant to Redline):** QA processes have been part of the
CAS renewal documentation for approximately 6-8 years. Firms are asked to outline
their basic QA measures at renewal. The original intent was to drive discipline around:
not signing up to bespoke contract conditions; having upfront scope conversations;
using industry standard conditions; generating internal review processes and checking
of calculations and drawings.

**AI dimension:** No AI-related PI claims in NZ engineering to date (as of May 2026).
AON's Claims Manager expects them in the future. The most common AI-related claims
to date are against lawyers (fabricated case law citations). Craig Lewis's standard
advice: "Treat AI like a bright, very keen young graduate. It doesn't excuse lack of
checking... the buck stops with whoever's signing the producer statements."

### 2. Public Liability (PL) / General Liability

**What it covers:** Accidental bodily injury to third parties or physical property
damage. Complements PI — PL handles physical damage, PI handles pure financial loss.

**Policy type:** Occurrence-based (not claims-made). Loss and claim occur within a
short period.

**Key features:**
- Excess: $500 per claim
- Limits: $1M–$10M (higher available on request)
- Average premiums: $500–$3,000 per annum for small-to-medium firms
- ACC (NZ's no-fault scheme) eliminates much bodily injury risk for consulting engineers

**Recommended minimum (per Craig Lewis and Christine Crook):** $2M–$5M. Trend is
moving away from $1M as a sufficient minimum. $5M is increasingly "the new one" in
current contract terms.

**Typical claim scenarios:**
- Accidentally damaging equipment on a building site
- Causing damage to a building or tenancy requiring demolition and rebuild

### 3. Statutory Liability

**What it covers:** Regulatory action from unintentional breaches of NZ statutes.
Primary value is funding legal defense costs for regulatory investigations.

**Key features:**
- Policy limits: $250K–$1M+ with low $500 excess
- Premiums: under $1,000 for small-to-mid firms
- NZ statute only (overseas work requires separate discussion)
- **Cannot insure** fines under the Health and Safety at Work Act or Resource
  Management Act (by law)
- Can cover fines and reparations where law permits, plus legal defense costs

**Regulatory context:** WorkSafe has recorded 600+ successful prosecutions since the
current health and safety regime. 83% of prosecutions result in parties being held
accountable. Complex/hazardous projects carry higher regulatory exposure.

### 4. Cyber Liability

**What it covers:** Response costs, data restoration, third-party claims, and business
interruption from cyber events.

**Key features:**
- Two standard limit options; excess: $25,000
- Premiums: $750–$2,500 for mid-sized firms
- Tripartite response: insurer's IT forensic panel + policyholder's local IT provider
- **Coverage conditions:** Basic internal risk management required — passwords,
  encryption, multi-factor authentication. Cover restricted if these are absent.

**Typical scenarios:** Ransomware encrypting design servers or document management
systems; email compromise leading to fraudulent payment instructions; loss of
confidential client data.

### 5. Management Liability (Umbrella)

Three sub-categories:

**5a. Employers Liability**
- Covers employee injuries outside ACC (mental injuries, gradual process injuries)
- NZ jurisdiction only
- Low excess and premium

**5b. Employment Practices Liability (EPL)**
- Covers personal grievances, unjustified dismissal, discrimination, harassment
- Covers past, present, and prospective employees
- Excess: $25,000 per claim; higher frequency than other covers
- **Critical requirement:** Employer must seek professional advice *before* taking
  disciplinary action for the policy to respond

**5c. Directors and Officers (D&O)**
- Protects directors against personal liability for mismanagement, governance
  failures, Companies Act breaches
- Side A: no excess — personal defense costs, personal assets protected
- Side B: $75,000 excess — company reimburses director
- Restriction: actions by 15%+ shareholder against a fellow director excluded
- Available limits: $500K–$1M+

## Q&A — Full Record (Private/Internal Use Only)

### Q1 — Named clients on public liability policies
**Asked by:** Craig Lewis (CAS Chair)

**Question:** Clients increasingly want to be named on a public liability policy —
not just confirmation of cover, but personally named. What are the pitfalls?

**Christine Crook (AON):** The PL policy has provision to include this, but the client
cannot be named to the same extent as the policyholder. They can be treated as covered
for liability attaching to them *as a direct result of your actions*. This means if they
receive a third-party property damage claim arising from your actions, they can access
your policy directly without having to indemnify the claimant and then seek recovery from
you. "The devil's in the detail of the contract — always good to refer to us to make sure
it aligns with that clause before agreeing to it in contract."

---

### Q2 — How to choose the PI cover level
**Asked by:** Read by Michelle from online forum

**Christine Crook:** Depends on: project complexity, construction value, timeline,
contract conditions, practice size (accumulation factor), and personal risk appetite.
Key points: you don't have to disclose your PI level to the client; certificates of
insurance can show lower limits than your actual cover.

**Craig Lewis:** Also depends on nature of work — residential work under the Consumer
Guarantees Act (where liability can't be limited) should trigger higher cover. At renewal,
seek options for alternative levels — increasing the elected excess can fund a significant
increase in PI cover at modest cost. Noted the impact of construction inflation: a claim
that seems small in 2026 may be far more expensive to resolve in 6-10 years if it
manifests later.

---

### Q3 — Renewing short form agreements annually
**Asked by:** Michelle (from online forum)

**Craig Lewis:** Depends on continuity and nature of recurring work. Generally better
to have a separate short form agreement for each piece of work. Perpetual agreements
carry risk — only the client can cancel, not the engineer.

---

### Q4 — NZ-based engineer working on NZ projects from overseas
**Asked by:** Michelle (from online forum)

**Christine Crook:** Requires a discussion with your adviser. Disclosure issues around
maintaining current with NZ regulations and standards. Employers Liability and
Employment Disputes are NZ-jurisdiction only. Australia requires local policies for
AU operations (AON can assist via AU colleagues).

### Q5 — How PI renewal addresses internal QA processes *(REDLINE-RELEVANT)*
**Asked by:** Read by Michelle from online forum

**Question (verbatim):** "How do PI renewal conversations typically address the firm's
internal QA processes? Is that a factor in underwriting or is it mostly about claims
history?"

**Christine Crook (verbatim):** *"So yes, it is definitely a factor in underwriting...
we do try and extract some of those questions from you at renewal time as well."*

**Craig Lewis (verbatim):** *"Yeah, it became a part of our renewal documentation
probably six or eight years ago... where our co-insurers were going through a
particularly painful period and we made a requirement for members as part of the
renewal documents to outline your basic QA measures... it was really to drive the
discipline of not signing up to bespoke conditions... making sure that you had
upfront conversations with your clients about scope... signed up to the industry
standard conditions... and just generating good internal QA processes of internal
reviews and decent checking of calculations and drawings... it should be part of
everyone's renewal processes to just review that and make sure that your individual
firm's QA processes are still fit for purpose."*

**Interpretation (Ron, internal):** This directly validates the bifurcation thesis.
QA documentation is confirmed as a renewal-time input, not just claims history.
The fact that it has been in place for 6-8 years means this is established practice
at CAS/NZI — not an emerging experiment.

### Q6 — AI claims in engineering reports *(REDLINE-RELEVANT)*
**Asked by:** Read by Michelle from online forum

**Question (verbatim):** "Are you seeing PI claims where AI-generated content in
engineering reports was a contributing factor, or is it still theoretical?"

**Gaynor Roberts, Claims Manager (verbatim):** *"Um, no, we're not, but we're
expecting to... we haven't actually seen [AI claims] even across other schemes...
The most frequent claims would be against lawyers because lawyers tend to use AI
to look for case law and that can be easily fabricated... it's definitely going to be
an exposure in the future, but we're not seeing it on the engineers book."*

**Craig Lewis (verbatim):** *"Treat AI like a bright, very keen young graduate. It
doesn't excuse lack of checking... at the end of the day it comes down to the internal
QA processes and the buck stops with whoever's signing the producer statements...
doesn't absolve you from thoroughly checking the accuracy of AI and weeding out all
the hallucinations."*

**Interpretation (Ron, internal):** Redline is ahead of the wave. The insurer expects
AI claims but hasn't seen them yet. Craig Lewis's "treat AI like a keen young graduate"
framing is the current practitioner standard — which Redline's pre-review QA layer is
designed to operationalise systematically.

### Q7 — Choosing PL cover limit
**Asked by:** Michelle (from online forum)

**Craig Lewis:** Everyone should carry $2M–$5M minimum. $10M isn't exorbitant for
larger projects.

**Christine Crook:** $5M is becoming "the new one" — client contract driven. $2M–$5M
more appropriate in today's higher-cost environment.

---

### Q8 — Commercial agreements with external IT providers re: cyber security
**Asked by:** Michelle (from online forum)

**Christine Crook:** Always have agreements with IT providers covering business
continuity and availability. Tripartite approach: insurer's forensic panel + your
IT provider work together. Caution: many firms have an IT person whose scope is
narrow (website, content only) — insufficient capacity for a major cyber event.

### Q10 — Would systematic AI QA processes get more favorable underwriting? *(REDLINE-RELEVANT)*
**Asked by:** Read by Michelle from online forum

**Question (verbatim):** "Would firms with systematic AI QA processes get more
favorable underwriting treatment?"

**Craig Lewis (verbatim):** *"I think that would be something that NZI would need
to consider... On renewal, would have to be a fairly good argument put forward,
I would have thought."*

**Christine Crook (verbatim):** *"Yes. Yeah. AI obviously presents a lot of solutions
and efficiencies but as with most things it's about also understanding what the
downside might be and what the risk exposure might be. So for insurance, AI is really

the risk profile it presents as well. So I would say that this is part of the evolution

Neither confirmed nor denied. "NZI would need to consider" and "part of the evolution"
are holding-pattern answers designed not to create policy expectations in a public
setting. The coffee meeting with Christine Crook is still the right path for a candid
answer. Revised coffee meeting question: *"When a firm has an actual documented QA
process — a written checklist with a sign-off trail, not just 'seniors review
everything' — does that show up anywhere in the renewal conversation, or is it
essentially invisible to underwriting?"*
