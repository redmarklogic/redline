# Problem Diagnosis in Good Strategy, Bad Strategy

*Source: Rumelt, R. (2011). Good Strategy Bad Strategy. Crown Business.*

---

## What Is a Problem Diagnosis?

A diagnosis is the **first and foundational element** of the kernel of good strategy - the structure that every real strategy must contain:

1. **Diagnosis** - defines or explains the nature of the challenge
2. **Guiding policy** - the overall approach to dealing with the challenge
3. **Coherent actions** - concrete steps that carry out the guiding policy

A diagnosis answers one core question: **"What is going on here?"**

It is not a list of goals. It is not a performance target. It is a **judgment about the meaning of facts** - a simplified story that identifies which aspects of reality are critical and deserve the most attention.

> "At a minimum, a diagnosis names or classifies the situation, linking facts into patterns and suggesting that more attention be paid to some issues and less to others."
> - Rumelt

---

## Why Is Diagnosis Important?

### 1. Strategy without diagnosis is not strategy

A strategy is a way *through an obstacle*. If you haven't identified the obstacle, you have a budget, a wish list, or a stretch goal - not a strategy.

> "If you fail to identify and analyze the obstacles, you don't have a strategy."

### 2. Diagnosis defines the domain of action

A good diagnosis zeroes in on what promises **leverage** - not merely what best predicts outcomes. It narrows where to focus energy, eliminating the illusion that you must improve everything simultaneously.

### 3. Diagnosis enables evaluation and adaptation

Making the diagnosis explicit allows the rest of the strategy to be judged and revised as circumstances change. Without a stated diagnosis, it is impossible to know whether a strategy is working or why it is failing.

### 4. Diagnosis unlocks analogical knowledge

When a diagnosis classifies a situation as a particular *type*, it opens access to how similar situations were handled historically. A novel-looking problem that maps onto a known class becomes tractable.

### 5. Diagnosis can transform perspective entirely

The most powerful diagnoses don't just name the situation - they reframe it so radically that an entirely different set of actions becomes available.

---

## The Core Pitfall: Confusing Results With Causes

**Bad diagnosis** names a result and calls it the problem.

> "When a leader characterizes the challenge as underperformance, it sets the stage for bad strategy. Underperformance is a result. The true challenges are the reasons for the underperformance."

Unless a diagnosis offers a *theory of why* - why things haven't worked, why the challenge is hard - it cannot generate a coherent response. It merely reframes failure as a target.

---

## Principles of Good Problem Diagnosis

### Principle 1: Look past results to root causes

Ask *why* performance is what it is, not merely *what* performance is. A result (low market share, poor test scores, declining revenue) is the symptom. The diagnosis must explain the mechanism producing it.

**Test:** Can you explain what structural or situational forces are causing the result? If not, you have described a symptom, not a diagnosis.

### Principle 2: Simplify reality into a useful story

Reality is overwhelmingly complex. A diagnosis replaces this complexity with a *simpler narrative* - one that identifies the leverage points. The goal is not completeness; it is actionability.

**Test:** Does the diagnosis tell you where to look first and what to ignore?

### Principle 3: Make an educated guess

Strategic challenges are often **ill-structured**: there is no obvious list of solutions, outcomes are unclear, and facts underdetermine the right interpretation. A diagnosis is not deduced - it is a *judgment* about what is critically important.

> "A diagnosis had to be an educated guess as to what was going on in the situation."

Do not wait for certainty. Make the best diagnosis the evidence supports, make it explicit, and treat it as revisable.

### Principle 4: Diagnose for leverage, not for accuracy

A social scientist seeks the explanation that best predicts outcomes. A strategist seeks the explanation that opens up the most *actionable* response. These are different objectives.

**Test:** Does the diagnosis point toward something that can actually be changed or addressed?

### Principle 5: Link facts into patterns

A diagnosis is not a single observation - it is a classification that connects multiple facts into a coherent picture. Look for patterns across data points, not isolated anomalies.

**Test:** Would your diagnosis hold if you added or removed any single data point? Robust diagnoses rest on a pattern, not a single fact.

### Principle 6: Make the diagnosis explicit

An unstated diagnosis cannot be examined, challenged, or improved. Explicit diagnosis allows others to test it, and allows the strategy to evolve as conditions change.

## How to Diagnose: Practical Guidance

### Step 1: Ask "What is going on here?" - repeatedly

Treat this as the primary question. Resist the urge to jump to solutions. Keep asking until you have an explanation, not just a description.

### Step 2: Separate results from causes

List what you observe (low growth, poor quality, talent loss). Then for each, ask: *What is producing this?* Push one level deeper each time until you reach something structural and addressable.

### Step 3: Classify the situation

What *type* of problem is this? Examples of useful classifications:
- Coordination failure vs. capability gap
- Competitive advantage erosion vs. market shift
- Execution failure vs. strategic error
- Incentive misalignment vs. missing information

Classification opens historical analogies and proven response patterns.

### Step 4: Work backward from proposed actions (Problem-Solution technique)

If a guiding policy already exists, ask: *What problem would this policy be the right answer to?* This reverse-engineering approach often surfaces the implicit diagnosis and allows you to make it explicit and testable.

> "Shift your attention from what is being done to why it is being done, from the directions chosen to the problems that these choices address."

### Step 5: Identify the "elephant in the elevator"

What is the central obstacle that everyone knows exists but no one is naming in the plan? Bad strategy often produces voluminous documents that carefully avoid the real problem.

**Ask:** What are we *not* talking about that would make the most difference?

### Step 6: Check that the diagnosis promises leverage

Before committing to a diagnosis, ask: Does this point toward something we can actually affect? If the diagnosis is correct but leads nowhere actionable, it may be technically accurate but strategically useless.

## Illustrative Examples

### Good Diagnosis: IBM (1993)

**Situation:** IBM was in serious decline as the computer industry fragmented into chips, storage, software, and hardware niches. Dominant view: IBM's integrated model was obsolete.

**Gerstner's diagnosis:** IBM was the *only* company with expertise across all fragments. Its problem was not integration itself - it was *failing to use its integrated skills to solve customers' IT problems*. The obstacle was lack of internal coordination.

**Result:** Pivot from hardware to IT consulting. IBM's unique position became the foundation for the strategy rather than the liability.

*Why it was good:* Named the structural cause, reframed the situation entirely, pointed toward specific action (coordination, services orientation).

---

### Good Diagnosis: Kennan and the Cold War

**Situation:** The Soviet Union was expanding influence in the postwar world.

**Kennan's diagnosis:** The USSR was not an ordinary nation-state. Its leaders defined their mission as ideological opposition to capitalism. However, Soviet leaders were also pragmatic realists about power - they would respond to counterforce, not negotiation.

**Result:** The policy of "containment" - structured around this specific diagnosis of the adversary's nature.

*Why it was good:* Classified the adversary as a specific type (ideological expansionist + power realist), opening access to counterforce theory.

---

### Bad Diagnosis: International Harvester (1979)

**Situation:** One of America's largest industrial companies facing declining performance.

**What the plan said:** Increase market share in each division, cut costs across the board.

**What the plan ignored:** The "elephant in the elevator" - Harvester had the worst labour relations in American industry and grossly inefficient work organisation. Profit margins were half of competitors' for years.

**Result:** A six-month strike, $3 billion in losses, closure of 35 of 42 plants, collapse.

*Why it was bad:* The plan described desired outcomes (market share, lower costs) without ever diagnosing why performance was poor. The real obstacle was invisible in the document.

---

### Bad Diagnosis: LA Unified School District

**Situation:** 309 of 991 schools failing to meet standards.

**What the plan said:** The challenge is "underperformance." Goal: build transformational leadership.

**What was missing:** Any theory of *why* the schools were failing - the decades-long bureaucratic dysfunction, the socioeconomic conditions of the communities served, the lack of functional accountability.

**Result:** Blue-sky objectives with no path to achievement.

*Why it was bad:* "Underperformance" is the result, not the diagnosis. Without a theory of causes, the strategy is merely a restatement of the goal.

---

## Summary: Diagnostic Quality Checklist

| Question | Good Diagnosis | Bad Diagnosis |
|---|---|---|
| Does it explain *why* the situation is as it is? | Yes - names causes | No - names results |
| Does it classify the situation as a type? | Yes | No |
| Does it point toward leverage? | Yes - something actionable | No - or points at intractable causes |
| Is it explicit and testable? | Yes | No - implicit or aspirational |
| Does it name the real obstacle? | Yes - including the "elephant" | No - avoids the hard truth |
| Is it an educated judgment, not just data? | Yes | No - either no judgment or pure assertion |
