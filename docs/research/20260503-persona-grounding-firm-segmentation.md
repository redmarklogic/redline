# Persona Grounding: NZ/AU Geotechnical Firm Segmentation

**Date**: 2026-05-03
**Research question**: What do NZ/AU geotechnical consultancies look like at each size segment, and what structural characteristics of Small (5–50) firms make them the right beachhead for a quality-layer product?
**Actor**: Principal Geotechnical Engineer (Graeme) grounding customer personas for Ron and Mark
**Redline domains**: customer personas, quality layer, report review, standards compliance

---

## Summary

The four-segment model (Micro / Small / Medium / Large) aligns well with both the PSF management literature and my experience of the NZ/AU geotechnical market. The Small (5–50) segment is structurally the right beachhead: these firms have enough report volume to feel the review bottleneck acutely, but lack the budget for dedicated QA staff or internal R&D. Liability exposure is real and disproportionate at this size — a single PI claim can threaten the firm's existence. The talent shortage amplifies the bottleneck because there are not enough experienced seniors to spread the review load.

## 1. What Each Segment Looks Like in NZ/AU

### 1.1 Micro (1–4 technical staff)

**Archetype**: A solo CPEng (Chartered Professional Engineer) or a partnership of two principals, sometimes with one graduate and an admin.

**NZ/AU examples** *(practitioner knowledge)*:
- Dozens of these exist across NZ — often a principal who left a large firm after 20+ years. They trade under their own name or a small brand. Many are visible on the Engineering NZ CPEng register.
- UK parallel from notebooks: Remedy Geotechnics (grew from 2 to 4 engineering staff) [Source: Ground Engineering Magazine].

**Typical projects**: Residential subdivision consents, single-lot geotech assessments, retaining wall designs, slope stability reviews for resource consent. Occasionally subcontracted by larger firms for peer review or specialist opinion.

**Standards**: NZS 3604 (timber-framed buildings — triggers geotech assessment), NZS 4431 (earthworks), NZ Building Code B1 (structure), local council IDS (Infrastructure Design Standards), NZGS guidelines.

**Review process**: The principal IS the reviewer. There is no delegation possible because the principal wrote the report, or the one graduate wrote it and the principal reviews and signs. No independent review is feasible within the firm — the PSF literature notes that everyone in a small firm may have worked on the project, and they "must rely on past colleagues or mentors outside the firm to act as independent reviewers" [Source: PSF Management notebook, citation 18-19].

**QA system**: Informal to non-existent. Relies on the principal's experience and personal standards. The PSF literature says sole proprietors "lack the staff and personal time to devote to internal administrative projects like IDC (Internal Discipline and Control)" [Source: PSF Management notebook, citation 14-15].

**Redline relevance**: Low. Too few reports to justify a subscription. The principal does not need a tool to check their own work — they need billable hours. However, these firms are potential *referral sources* if they trust the tool from their time at larger firms.

### 1.2 Small (5–50 technical staff) — THE BEACHHEAD

**Archetype**: One to three principals/directors, two to five senior engineers (CPEng or working towards it), five to twenty intermediate engineers, two to five graduates, plus admin/drafting support.

**NZ/AU examples** *(practitioner knowledge, supplemented by notebook data)*:
- **NZ ~10-person firms**: Firms like HD Geo, Geotechnics, Earthtech Consulting, LDE, Eliot Sinclair's geotech team. Often single-office, one city. The founder knows every client personally.
- **NZ ~20-40 person firms**: Firms like Engeo NZ (multi-office, NZ/AU/US), CMW Geosciences, Riley Consultants, Ground Investigation Ltd. Multiple offices, starting to formalise systems.
- **AU equivalent**: Small-to-mid geotech firms like Douglas Partners (before it grew further), Geotesta, Pells Sullivan Meynink (PSM), Coffey's smaller regional offices (before WSP acquisition).
- **UK parallel from notebooks**: AGS Ground Solutions (11 people, ceased trading), Piledesigns (11 engineers, 1,700+ instructions per year) [Source: Ground Engineering Magazine].

**Typical projects**: Commercial and industrial foundations, multi-storey residential, subdivisions, retaining walls, slope stability for infrastructure, preliminary GIRs for roading/rail, ground investigation management, earthworks supervision and testing. Some firms specialise (e.g., contaminated land, seismic assessment). Revenue typically NZ$1.5M–$15M.

**Standards**: B1/VM4, NZS 3604, NZS 4431, NZGS guidelines (slope stability, liquefaction, lateral spreading), NZ Transport Agency Bridge Manual, AS 2159 (piling — AU), AS 1726 (geotechnical site investigations — AU), Eurocode 7 (if working on international projects), local council IDS. The founder noted that standards "keep updating and frankly nobody remembers all of it" [Source: Founder Memos].

**Review process**: This is where the bottleneck bites. See Section 2 below.

**QA system**: Ranges from "we review each other's reports" (informal) to a documented checklist system. Very few have anything approaching the formal PD/TR/BIS system described in the Geotechnical Report Workflows notebook, which comes from a large NZ consultancy. The PSF literature confirms that smaller firms use "only an e-mail or short phone call between the engagement director and project manager for emerging issues... as well as a detailed, face-to-face review of handouts, draft deliverables, and final deliverables" [Source: PSF Management notebook, citation 17].

**Innovation investment**: Almost zero dedicated R&D budget. The PSF literature says small firms "invest almost exclusively in 'base technologies' — technologies strictly necessary to do business, like word processing and CADD" and that "pacing technologies (experimental, cutting-edge, or high-risk R&D innovations) are generally viewed as too expensive" [Source: PSF Management notebook, citations 26-28]. The founder confirms: small firms "don't have the budget for R&D" [Source: Founder Memos].

### 1.3 Medium (50–500 technical staff)

**Archetype**: Multiple practice teams (geotech, structural, water, transport), regional offices, practice leads, possibly a dedicated QA manager. Starting to have formal management layers.

**NZ/AU examples** *(practitioner knowledge)*:
- **NZ**: Tonkin + Taylor (~800 staff total, ~300+ geotech), Beca (multidisciplinary, large geotech team), GHD's NZ geotech practice, WSP NZ.
- **AU**: Coffey (now WSP), Douglas Partners (~600 staff), Golder (now WSP), SMEC, Aurecon's geotech divisions.
- **UK parallel**: Concept Engineering Consultants (110 employees) [Source: Ground Engineering Magazine].

**Typical projects**: Major infrastructure (roads, rail, tunnels, dams), large subdivisions, complex slope remediation, contaminated land assessment, seismic hazard studies, GBRs for tunnelling, expert witness work.

**QA system**: Formal. The large NZ consultancy whose procedures populate our Geotechnical Report Workflows notebook operates at this scale — with defined PD (Project Director), TR (Technical Reviewer), BIS (Business Integrated Services), component reviews, and mandatory sign-off forms [Source: Geotechnical Report Workflows notebook]. "The PD and TR must be independent of the presentation of the work package" [Source: Geotechnical Report Workflows notebook, citation 9]. They use structured checklists — including specialist ones for slope stability modelling that verify "model geometry, geological unit boundaries, material parameters (cohesion, friction angle), groundwater/piezometric conditions, and seismic accelerations" [Source: Geotechnical Report Workflows notebook, citation 19].

**Redline relevance**: These firms may be early majority adopters but are NOT the beachhead. They already have formal QA. They are more likely to build internal AI experiments. However, their middle-tier engineers (who do the actual writing) would benefit from the tool — the challenge is selling to the management layer that already invested in manual QA.

### 1.4 Large (500+ technical staff)

**Archetype**: Global or national firms with dedicated R&D, legal, QA, and business development teams.

**NZ/AU examples** *(practitioner knowledge)*:
- **NZ/AU**: AECOM, WSP, Jacobs, Aurecon, GHD (at group level). These are the multinationals.
- **UK from notebooks**: RSK Group (1,505 staff), Fugro (680), AECOM (610), Mott MacDonald (350) [Source: Ground Engineering Magazine].

**The founder's observation**: Large firms "have dedicated teams for bidding, dedicated team for legal" and "budget for R&D so they can develop tools or different ways of working" [Source: Founder Memos]. They "have no incentive to sell" their internal tools because "this gives them an advantage" [Source: Founder Memos].

**Redline relevance**: Not a target. They build bespoke. They are potential *competitors* if they ever productise their internal tools, but the founder correctly identifies that they have no incentive to do so [Source: Founder Memos].

## 2. The Reviewer Bottleneck in Small Firms (5–50)

### 2.1 How the Senior Review Process Actually Works Day-to-Day

In my experience across 25 years, the typical review process in a 10–30 person NZ geotech firm works like this:

1. **Graduate/intermediate writes the report** — typically using a previous report from a similar project as a template. They copy the structure, adapt the site description, paste in the new borehole logs and lab results, update the analysis, and write recommendations.

2. **Author does a cursory self-check** — maybe a spell-check and a read-through. Rarely a systematic checklist. The Geotechnical Report Workflows notebook describes a "20 Checkpoints in 90 Seconds" self-review tool, but this comes from a large firm; small firms almost never have such tools [Source: Geotechnical Report Workflows notebook, citation 10-12].

3. **Report lands on a senior's desk** — the senior might be one of two or three CPEng holders in the firm. The report arrives as a Word document, usually with a request like "can you have a look at this when you get a chance?"

4. **Senior reviews when they can** — this is the bottleneck. The senior is also managing their own projects, writing proposals, visiting clients, doing site visits, mentoring graduates, and handling admin. Report review gets squeezed into gaps. Friday afternoon is common. Late at night is not unusual.

5. **Red pen or Track Changes** — the senior reads the report, marks up corrections in Track Changes or printed with red pen. Comments range from "fix this typo" to "your bearing capacity calculation is wrong — redo it" to "you haven't addressed liquefaction at all."

6. **Back to the author for corrections** — the cycle repeats. Two to three rounds is normal for a complex report from a graduate. One round for a competent intermediate.

7. **Senior signs off** — the senior (usually a CPEng) reviews the final version and authorises issue. In NZ, the CPEng's signature carries personal liability.

### 2.2 Report Volume Per Senior Reviewer

This is hard to quantify precisely, but from my experience:

- A busy senior in a small firm reviews **3–8 reports per week**, depending on complexity. Simple residential assessments (3–5 pages) take 15–30 minutes to review. A 40-page GIR for a commercial development might take 2–4 hours across two review rounds.
- In a 15-person firm with two CPEng seniors, each senior is reviewing work from 5–7 intermediates/graduates. That means **15–25 reports per month per senior** that need substantive technical review, plus perhaps 20+ minor reviews (lab reports, field notes, correspondence).
- **The math does not work.** If each substantive review takes 1–3 hours (including reading, marking up, and discussing with the author), that is 15–75 hours per month just on review — on top of the senior's own project work, business development, and management responsibilities.

### 2.3 Most Common Quality Failures That Slip Through

From the notebooks and my experience, these are the errors that most frequently survive review or get caught too late:

**Technical errors:**
- Calculation errors in bearing capacity, settlement, or slope stability — "No one is error-free but an adequate system of independent checks should catch the most flagrant technical errors" [Source: Risk Assessment notebook, citation 7].
- Wrong design parameters used (e.g., using lab peak strength instead of residual strength for a slope with pre-existing shear surfaces).
- Liquefaction assessment omitted or superficial for sites that clearly require it.
- Groundwater conditions not adequately characterised or ignored in analysis.

**Administrative and formatting errors:**
- Template/copy-paste errors: "Blindly reusing previous or example reports without tailoring the structure to the new project's context, leading to illogical flows or inappropriate assumptions" [Source: Geotechnical Report Workflows notebook, citation 22-24].
- Inconsistent tenses — "Mixing past and future tenses confusingly (e.g., describing completed fieldwork in the future tense)" [Source: Geotechnical Report Workflows notebook, citation 20-21].
- Previous project name left in the report header or body (a classic copy-paste failure).
- Missing or incorrect standard references.
- Conclusions that introduce new information not supported by the preceding text [Source: Geotechnical Report Workflows notebook, citation 5, 21].
- Missing scope limitations, caveats, or applicability statements.

**Standards compliance gaps:**
- Wrong or outdated version of NZS 4431 or IDS referenced.
- Seismic design parameters not updated to current NZS 1170.5 or NZTA Bridge Manual.
- Council-specific requirements missed (each council in NZ has slightly different IDS requirements).

### 2.4 Informal QA vs. Formal QA

**Informal QA (typical in Small firms):**
- No written QA procedure document.
- Review is ad hoc: "the senior reads it before it goes out."
- No mandatory checklist — the senior relies on experience and memory.
- No independence requirement — the project manager may also be the reviewer.
- No sign-off form — just an email saying "looks good, send it."
- No post-project review or lessons-learned process.

**Formal QA (typical in Medium/Large firms):**
- Written Quality Management System (QMS), often ISO 9001 certified.
- Defined roles: PD (oversight), TR (technical review), PM (project management), with independence rules [Source: Geotechnical Report Workflows notebook].
- Mandatory checklists — including discipline-specific ones for slope stability, foundation design, etc.
- Sign-off forms that require PD and TR signatures before issue [Source: Geotechnical Report Workflows notebook, citation 16-18].
- Post-project reviews and engagement evaluations [Source: PSF Management notebook, citation 18].

**The gap that Redline fills**: Small firms operate with informal QA because they cannot justify the overhead of formal QA. A quality-layer tool provides the *benefits* of formal QA (systematic checking, standards compliance verification, consistency checking) without the *cost* of dedicated QA staff and bureaucratic processes.

## 3. Liability vs. Talent as Priority #2

### 3.1 Ron's Argument: Liability Is #2

Ron argues that liability is the second-highest priority for small firms (after financial performance) because they have zero buffer against a single PI claim. **I largely agree, with a nuance.**

### 3.2 Evidence on Liability Exposure

**Claims frequency is high and rising:**
- In Canada, claim frequency increased from one claim per 20 firms in 1966 to **one claim for every 3 firms since 1978** [Source: Risk Assessment notebook, citation 5].
- "Professional liability cases are like aircraft accidents — for every disaster there are probably a hundred 'near misses'" [Source: Risk Assessment notebook, citation 6].
- "Amounts claimed in suits are disproportionate to consultants' project fee revenue" [Source: Risk Assessment notebook, citation 4].

**Design errors dominate:**
- 52% of claims allege design errors [Source: Risk Assessment notebook, citation 9].
- Over 50% of claims contain allegations of negligence in field services (compaction control, soil inspection) [Source: Risk Assessment notebook, citations 11-12].

**PI insurance is structurally painful for small firms:**
- Premiums are calculated as a rate multiplied by estimated gross fees [Source: Risk Assessment notebook, citations 1-3]. This means it is a direct percentage of revenue — not a fixed cost that scales with firm size.
- "Even minor disputes cost at least $10,000 from profits" [Source: CEO Priorities knowledge document, citation from Risk Assessment notebook].
- Insurers evaluate QA/QC systems when setting premiums — "it is beneficial to risk management if the insured has procedures to check the quality of designs" [Source: Risk Assessment notebook, citation 15].
- From the UK: PI cover is being reduced to "aggregate basis" (capped total for all claims) and limits slashed from £10M to £2M while premiums rise. This "restricts SMEs from being able to bid on larger projects" [Source: Ground Engineering Magazine].

**NZ/AU-specific context** *(practitioner knowledge — I flag this as unverified by notebooks)*:
- In NZ, PI insurance for a small geotech firm typically runs **3–8% of gross revenue**, depending on project types and claims history. Firms doing higher-risk work (tunnelling, dam design) pay more. A 10-person firm billing NZ$2M might pay $60K–$160K in PI premiums.
- In my experience, **near-misses are far more common than formal claims**. A "near-miss" might be: a retaining wall designed without adequate drainage fails during a heavy rain event, but the homeowner does not pursue a claim because the remediation cost was absorbed. These near-misses erode profit margins without triggering the insurance system.
- A single significant claim — say NZ$500K — can wipe out a small firm's profit for 2–3 years and make future insurance unaffordable. The firm may survive the claim but lose the ability to bid on work that requires adequate PI cover.

### 3.3 The Talent Dimension

The talent shortage is equally acute but manifests differently:

**From the notebooks:**
- The "people crisis": scarcity, high cost, and difficulty of retaining mid-level professionals. "People join firms for careers, not jobs" [Source: PSF Management notebook, citation 11-12].
- Leverage (ratio of junior to senior staff) drives profitability, but "systemic underdelegation — where senior staff do junior work — destroys profit, demotivates juniors, and traps seniors away from business development" [Source: CEO Priorities knowledge document].
- The UK geotechnical industry is suffering from a "chronic shortage of graduates and skilled staff." Small firms rely on government-funded training programmes and proposed cuts threaten their ability to train engineers [Source: Ground Engineering Magazine, citations 13-15].

**My assessment for a 10-person firm's principal:**
- **Both are acutely felt, but differently.** The talent shortage is a *chronic* pain — it is always there, always discussed, always a constraint. You cannot hire fast enough, and when you do hire, you spend 2–3 years developing a graduate before they can produce work that does not require heavy review.
- Liability is an *acute* pain — it is episodic and terrifying. Most of the time, a principal does not think about it day-to-day. But when a claim lands, or when the PI renewal comes in 20% higher, or when a project goes wrong and the lawyer's letter arrives, it becomes the only thing that matters.
- **The connection between them is the key insight**: the talent shortage *causes* the liability exposure. When you have too many intermediates per senior, review quality drops. When review quality drops, errors slip through. When errors slip through, claims happen. The quality layer addresses both problems simultaneously — it reduces the review burden (talent) and catches errors before issue (liability).

**My verdict**: Ron is right that liability is #2, but the framing should be: **liability is the *consequence* of the talent problem, and a quality-layer tool sits at the intersection of both.**

## 4. What the Quality Layer Needs to Check

Based on the GBR notebook, the Geotechnical Report Workflows notebook, and my experience, here is what a useful pre-review checklist would look like for a Small firm's typical report output.

### 4.1 For GIR / General Geotechnical Reports

**Structural / completeness checks:**
- [ ] Report purpose and scope clearly stated and aligned with the LOE (Letter of Engagement)
- [ ] Site description includes location, legal description, proposed development
- [ ] Ground investigation scope adequately described (boreholes, CPTs, test pits — with locations)
- [ ] Ground conditions summarised with a coherent ground model
- [ ] Groundwater conditions described and assessed
- [ ] Seismic hazard assessment included (NZS 1170.5 parameters, liquefaction assessment if required)
- [ ] Foundation recommendations include type, depth, bearing capacity, and settlement estimates
- [ ] Earthworks recommendations include compaction requirements (NZS 4431 reference)
- [ ] Slope stability addressed if relevant
- [ ] Limitations, caveats, and applicability statements included
- [ ] Conclusions do not introduce new information
- [ ] References are complete and cite current standard versions

**Standards compliance checks:**
- [ ] Correct version of NZS 1170.5 (seismic) referenced
- [ ] Correct local council IDS referenced (and correct version)
- [ ] NZS 4431 earthworks requirements correctly applied
- [ ] NZGS guidelines referenced where applicable (liquefaction, slope stability, etc.)
- [ ] NZ Building Code B1/VM4 requirements met

**Consistency checks:**
- [ ] Project name, address, and client name consistent throughout (catch copy-paste errors)
- [ ] Borehole/CPT numbers match between text, figures, and appendices
- [ ] Design parameters used in analysis match those reported in the ground model
- [ ] Conclusions consistent with analysis results
- [ ] No previous project references left in the template

**Language and clarity checks:**
- [ ] Consistent tense throughout
- [ ] No ambiguous language ("may," "could," "should" in definitive statements)
- [ ] Sentences of reasonable length
- [ ] Audience-appropriate tone (consent application vs. detailed design report)

### 4.2 For GBR (Geotechnical Baseline Reports)

In addition to the above, the GBR notebook identifies these critical checks:

- [ ] **No GIR confusion**: GBR is the only interpretive report for bidding/construction — "Preparing a GIR alongside a GBR is superfluous, a potential source of confusion and conflict, and is strongly discouraged" [Source: GBR notebook]
- [ ] **No taboo words**: Baseline statements must use definitive verbs ("is," "will," "are") — flag any use of "may," "can," "might," "up to," "could," "should," "would," "some," "few," "ranges from... to..." [Source: GBR notebook]
- [ ] **No "shall"**: The GBR is not a specification. "Shall" must be removed [Source: GBR notebook]
- [ ] **No soft baselines**: Baselines must specify definitive values, not full data ranges [Source: GBR notebook]
- [ ] **No two-tier baselines**: No mixing of "contractually binding" and "for information only" statements [Source: GBR notebook]
- [ ] **No specification repetition**: GBR must not repeat technical specifications or drawings [Source: GBR notebook]
- [ ] **Measurability**: Every baseline must be measurable in the field with practical methods [Source: GBR notebook]
- [ ] **Deviation rationale**: Any baseline that deviates from GDR factual data must explain why [Source: GBR notebook]
- [ ] **Length check**: Flag if GBR exceeds 30–50 pages (excluding figures) [Source: GBR notebook]

## 5. Other Structural Characteristics Affecting Adoption

### 5.1 Factors Favouring Adoption

1. **Agility**: SMEs "bring new innovations to market much faster than larger, bureaucratic mega-firms" [Source: Ground Engineering Magazine]. A 15-person firm can adopt a new tool in a week if the principal decides to try it.

2. **The "race to the bottom" in procurement**: Small firms compete on price, leading to tight margins [Source: Ground Engineering Magazine, citation 18]. Any tool that saves time on the review cycle directly improves margins.

3. **Word-native workflow**: Engineers work in Word. The founder notes this explicitly — "most of the engineering work is done in word documents and they add comments in word documents" [Source: Founder Memos]. A quality layer that works within or alongside Word will have lower adoption friction than one that requires a separate platform.

4. **Trust through transparency**: Engineers "need to trust the model" and "need to see that it uses the right standards" [Source: Founder Memos, citations 12-13]. A checklist-style output (rather than a black-box rewrite) aligns with engineering culture.

5. **Marketing angle — PI insurance**: If Redline can demonstrate reduced error rates, it becomes a talking point for PI insurance renewal. Insurers evaluate QA systems when setting premiums [Source: Risk Assessment notebook, citation 15]. "We use Redline as part of our QA process" is a sentence a principal would want to say to their insurer.

### 5.2 Factors Creating Resistance

1. **Reviewer ego** *(see existing knowledge document: [memo-positioning-language-reviewer-sensitivity.md](docs/knowledge/geotechnical/report-writing/memo-positioning-language-reviewer-sensitivity.md))*: "The line 'catches what a senior reviewer would catch' tells a Technical Director that their reviewers are missing things. TDs built those review processes. They trained those people. You are telling them their house is not in order."

2. **"We've always done it this way"**: Small geotech firms are conservative. Many principals are 50+ years old and have been doing manual review for decades. The literature notes that "the greatest source of resistance to change might be you (and your partners if you have them). Fight against personal and firm inertia" [Source: PSF Management notebook, citation 15].

3. **Data sensitivity**: Geotechnical reports contain commercially sensitive information (site conditions, client names, project details). A principal will need assurance that report data is not stored, shared, or used for training. The founder acknowledges this: "we don't save the data and all this" [Source: Founder Memos].

4. **Cost sensitivity**: Small firms spend 1–4.3% of gross revenue on marketing [Source: PSF Management notebook, citation 24]. Their overhead tolerance for non-essential tools is low. Pricing must be per-report or low monthly subscription, not enterprise SaaS pricing.

5. **Digital maturity varies wildly**: Some 15-person firms have a principal who built their own Excel-based calculation tools. Others still print reports and mark them up with red pen. "The digital maturity of the supply chain varies greatly. SMEs often feel daunted by the prospect of adopting cloud migrations or new software" [Source: Ground Engineering Magazine, citation 17].

6. **Lack of independent reviewer**: In a small firm, everyone worked on the project. The tool becomes valuable partly because it provides the "independent" check that the firm cannot do internally [Source: PSF Management notebook, citations 18-19]. But this also means the tool's output has no internal champion to validate it — the principal must trust the tool directly.

1. **NZ/AU-specific PI insurance data**: What proportion of a typical NZ geotechnical firm's overhead goes to PI insurance? I estimated 3–8% of revenue from my experience, but I have not verified this with published survey data. The ACENZ benchmarking surveys may contain this data.

2. **Leverage ratios by firm size**: The PSF literature discusses leverage conceptually but does not provide specific ratios by headcount tier. ACENZ or Engineers Australia salary surveys may provide data on the ratio of CPEng holders to total technical staff by firm size.

3. **Claims frequency in NZ specifically**: The one-in-three figure is from Canadian data (1978+). I do not have equivalent NZ statistics. Engineering NZ or the NZ insurance market may have this data.

4. **Named NZ firm examples**: I have used firm names from my practitioner knowledge. I have not verified current headcounts or revenue figures from public sources. Exact numbers should be confirmed before including in any published persona document.

5. **AU market structure**: The Australian geotechnical market is significantly larger than NZ's. The dynamics may differ — particularly regarding state-level regulatory variation (each Australian state has different planning and building regulations).
